I need to text Sarah. Her bio midterm is at nine. I told her I’d remind her to eat a banana before she goes in. 

The kettle is whistling. I can get it. I’m already up. 

I pour the water. Two mugs. Peppermint for Jess, chamomile for me. You have to let it steep for exactly four minutes or it gets that bitter taste at the back of your throat. I pull the sleeves of my sweater down over my knuckles because the ceramic is hot, but I like that it’s hot. I like holding something warm. My hands have been so cold lately. Just, completely freezing. I don’t know why.

I bring the mugs into the living room. Jess is sitting on the couch. My best friend. I’ve known her since we were eleven, since she fell off the swings and I gave her my jacket. She is looking at me right now like I am made of very thin, very cheap glass. 

I hate that she’s looking at me like that. It makes her shoulders tight. I don't like it when her shoulders are tight.

"Here," I say. I set the mug on the coaster in front of her. "It’s peppermint."

She doesn't look at the mug. She looks at my face, at the way my jaw is doing this weird trembling thing that I can't figure out how to turn off. "Riley," she says. Her voice is doing that careful thing. That slow, tiptoeing thing. 

"I have those pretzels," I say quickly. I turn around before she can reach out. If she touches me right now I am going to shatter all over the rug and then I’ll have to vacuum it up. "The yogurt-covered ones. I picked them up yesterday. I'll get them."

I walk back into the kitchen. I open the pantry. I stare at the bags. 

I just want everyone to be comfortable. That’s all I ever want. I just want the lighting to be warm and the playlist to be right—I should change the playlist, it’s too upbeat, it’s making the air in the room feel jagged. If I put on the acoustic mix, maybe Jess will relax. If Jess relaxes, I won't have to see the way my own ruined state is hurting her. 

I grab the pretzels. I pour them into the wooden bowl.

I grip the edge of the counter. The granite is freezing.

I take a breath. It catches on the way down, scraping against my ribs. I'm okay. I'm making a snack. I have to call my mom on Sunday. What am I going to tell my mom on Sunday? She always knows. She can hear it in the vowels of my hellos. I can't call her. But I have to call her, because if I don't, she'll worry, and I can't stand it when people worry. 

I pick up the wooden bowl. I walk back out. 

Mark is here now too. He came through the back door. He’s standing by the hallway, his jacket still on, his hands in his pockets. He looks at Jess. Jess looks at him. They are having a whole conversation over my head, passing my broken pieces back and forth with their eyes, completely terrified of dropping me. 

"Hey," I say. I smile. It hurts my cheeks. "I was just going to change the music. Do you want a water? You look thirsty."

"Riley, please," Mark says. He sounds like he’s choking. "Sit down. Please. You don't have to do this."

"I’m just getting water," I say. My voice is too high. It sounds like a bird trapped in a room. "Your cup is empty. I can see it from here. It's empty."

"I don't care about the cup," he says.

I step toward him. I want to take his coat. I want to hang it up so he can stay a while. I want to wrap them both in blankets and make sure they know they are loved, because I love them so much, and the world is so cold, and people pin you down to beds in dark rooms while the music thumps through the floorboards and they don't listen when you cry. 

I try to take his coat. My hands are shaking so badly I miss the zipper. 

"Riley," Jess whispers. She's standing up now.

"I just want to help," I say. I am crying. I didn't know I was crying until the salt hit my lips. "I just want to fix the room. It’s too bright in here. Why is it so bright in here?"

I back away from them. I bump into the coffee table. The peppermint tea spills over the rim of the mug, pooling dark and wet on the wood. 

I scramble to get a towel. I have to clean it up. I have to clean it up. I’m making a mess. I’m ruining the room. 

I sink onto my knees on the rug, scrubbing at the spilled tea with the sleeve of my sweater, but there's too much of it, and it's burning my wrist, and the music is too loud, and I can still feel his weight on my hips, crushing the breath out of me, pinning me to the mattress. 

I curl into myself on the floor, pulling my knees to my chest. I wrap my arms around my own shoulders. I am so cold.
