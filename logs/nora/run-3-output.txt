He’s reading the same paragraph he was reading an hour ago. His knees are pulled up to his chest. His elbows are tucked in tightly against his ribs. He is trying to become two-dimensional. 

Before October, his stuff was everywhere. He used to sprawl. He used to take up the whole center of the rug, lying on his back tossing a tennis ball at the ceiling. Now his sneakers are lined up perfectly under his bed frame. He doesn’t leave his jackets on my chair anymore. He has contained his entire existence to a three-by-six rectangle of mattress.

I can't watch him do it anymore. 

I stand up. 

He doesn't look at me, but his shoulders tense. He is waiting for me to announce my trajectory. To the door. To the mini-fridge. To the closet. So he can adjust.

I don't announce it. I just walk toward his bed.

He looks up then. His eyes widen, just a fraction. He instinctively shifts backward, his back hitting the cinderblock wall. He pulls his legs in tighter. There is nowhere left for him to go.

I keep walking. Over the center of the rug. 

My chest tries to lock. The air gets thin. My body says *danger, male, close, stop*. It fires the warning shots down my arms, making my fingers go cold. 

I ignore it. I look at his face. It’s Cal. It’s the guy who ate terrible dining hall waffles with me at 2 AM. It’s the guy who stood outside the administration building for three hours holding my backpack while I was inside talking to a panel of strangers. My mind knows this. I have to force my body to learn it again. 

I reach the edge of his bed. 

He is completely still. He looks terrified—not of me, but of breaking me. 

I sit down on the edge of his mattress. 

The springs creak. The bed dips, tilting us slightly toward each other. 

He doesn't speak. He barely breathes. His hands are flat on his textbook, his knuckles white. 

"You can take up space," I say. 

My voice shakes. Just a little. 

He swallows hard. "I'm fine, Nora."

"You're not." I look at his rigid shoulders. "Stop shrinking."

"I just... I don't want to crowd you."

"I know." 

I look at his hand on the textbook. I need to do this. I need to cross the wire. I cannot live the rest of my life in a body that flinches at my best friend.

I reach out. My hand hovers for a second. The warning alarms in my head are deafening. *Don't do it, don't let him, too close.* 

I push through the noise. I put my hand over his. 

His skin is warm. My hand is freezing. 

For a second, my muscles try violently to pull back. I force them to stay. I press my palm down against his knuckles. I anchor myself to him.

He looks at my hand, then up at my face. His eyes are bright. He doesn't move his hand to grab mine. He doesn't shift his weight. He just lets me hold his hand, keeping his own fingers perfectly still so I stay entirely in control. 

"I'm here," I say. 

He exhales. A long, shaky breath that seems to empty out his whole chest. His shoulders drop. They actually drop. 

"Okay," he says quietly. 

I don't let go. I sit on the edge of his bed, and I feel the pulse beating in his wrist, steady and safe, and I wait for my body to figure out that we are okay.My pulse is hitting the back of my teeth. 

I watch my own hand. It looks like it belongs to someone else. Pale and rigid, clamped over his like a vice. I am waiting for the urge to bolt to peak and break. 

He doesn't rush it. He doesn't say *it's okay*. He doesn't offer empty words. He just stays exactly where he is, anchoring his hand to the book, letting me use him as a grounding wire. 

The radiator hisses. 

Outside, a car door slams. 

My lungs pull in air. A real breath. Not the shallow, trapped kind, but a full, deep pull that reaches the bottom of my ribs. 

The steel door in my chest slides open an inch. 

I relax my grip. I don't let go, I just stop holding on so tightly. My palm rests flat against his. 

Cal feels the shift. I know he does, because he lets his head fall back against the cinderblock wall. He closes his eyes. The tight, careful mask he’s been wearing since October fractures, just for a second, and I see how incredibly tired he is. 

I did that. The thing that happened to me did that to him. 

"I hate it," I say. 

I didn't plan to speak. The words just fall out into the space between us. 

Cal opens his eyes. He looks at me. 

"I hate what it's making me do to you."

He finally moves. Slowly, telegraphing the motion so I have a full three seconds to pull away if I want to, he turns his hand over. Palm to palm. He doesn't lace our fingers. He just meets me halfway. 

"You're not doing it to me, Nora," he says. His voice is rough. "It was done to you. We're just dealing with it."

*We.*

Not just me. 

I look at our hands. There is no letter in the world that can un-write this. There is no panel of administrators who can sit at a long table and decide whether or not this room is real. They have their piece of paper in a drawer. I have this. 

I slide my thumb over his. 

The alarms in my head go quiet.[THAT'S ALL I HAVE]
