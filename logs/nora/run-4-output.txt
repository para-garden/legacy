His knees are pulled up. He’s taking up maybe two square feet of a mattress that belongs to him. He’s breathing in fractions. 

I am so tired of the math. I am so tired of this invisible tape on the floor.

I stand up. 

He looks up. He expects me to turn toward the door. Or the mini-fridge. He starts to shift his weight, recalculating his buffer zone.

I don't turn. I walk straight at him. 

Three steps. Four. I cross the border.

He freezes. His eyes get wide. He doesn't know what to do with his hands. 

I don't give myself a second to think about the geometry. I just sit down. Right on his lap. I throw my arms around his neck, I press my face into his shoulder, and I hold on.

Instantly, it starts. 

The white noise in my ears. The rushing water. Every alarm bell in my nervous system goes off at once. *Trap. Threat. Danger. Get out. Get out. Get out.* My lungs forget how to work. My spine turns into a steel rod. My skin crawls with ice. My body is frantically trying to eject me from this proximity. It is telling me I am dying.

I dig my fingers into the fabric of his t-shirt. I fist the cotton in both hands. 

I am not moving.

Cal is completely rigid beneath me. A statue. He’s not breathing. His hands are hovering somewhere in the air by my waist, terrified to land, terrified to touch me. He thinks he did something wrong. He thinks this is an accident, or that I'm sleepwalking, or that I'm breaking. 

*It’s Cal,* I tell my brain. *It’s Cal. It’s Cal.*

He smells like the peppermint soap from the dollar store. He smells like the library. He smells safe. 

My heart is battering against my ribs so hard I know he can feel it through our shirts. My whole body is vibrating with the effort of not bolting. I lock my wrists behind his neck. I press my forehead harder into his collarbone. 

*Stay,* I tell my muscles. *You are fine. Stay.*

"Nora," he whispers. His voice is wrecked. Panicked. 

I shake my head against his shoulder. I just squeeze tighter. 

*Hold on. Just hold on.*

Slowly. Centimeter by centimeter. I feel his arms come down. I feel his hands, light as paper, settle on my back. He doesn't pull me in. He doesn't trap me. He just lets me know they are there. A tentative, open-palmed pressure. 

I gasp for air. It’s a ragged, ugly sound. 

My body is screaming. It takes everything I have to keep my knees bent, to keep my arms locked, to stay anchored to him while every biological instinct begs me to run. But I am not letting the room stay divided anymore. I am not letting that piece of paper in the drawer dictate where I am allowed to sit. I am not letting him fold himself into nothing.

I am taking the hit. I am riding it out. 

"I've got you," he whispers into my hair. The words are shaking. "I'm right here. I've got you."

My eyes burn. My chest heaves. The panic is a live wire thrashing in my veins, but underneath it, buried deep beneath the noise and the freeze, is the solid, undeniable weight of my best friend. 

I close my eyes. I hold on.The roar in my ears is so loud it sounds like a train tunnel. 

I focus on his hands. They are planted right in the middle of my back. They aren't pressing. They aren't holding me down. If I shifted my weight, if I pushed back even an inch, his arms would drop. He would let me go instantly. 

That's the only reason I can stay. 

I count the beats of my heart. They are violent. Frantic against my ribs. 

Cal takes a breath. He draws it in slowly, and he lets it out slowly. He does it deliberately. Exaggerated. A metronome. He knows exactly what he’s doing. 

He takes another. His chest rises against mine. I feel the mechanics of it. Bone and muscle. Not a threat. Just Cal. Just the guy who eats dry cereal at two in the morning and leaves his textbooks open on the floor. 

*Breathe in.* 

I try to match his rhythm. I only get a thimbleful of air, catching in my throat. 

*Breathe out.*

He does it again. I trace the smell of peppermint soap. I trace the woven seams of his shirt. 

The live wire in my veins sparks, snaps, and then, very slowly, it begins to hum. 

The panic starts to run out of fuel. Adrenaline can only burn for so long before it eats itself. The ice in my skin melts into a heavy, aching exhaustion. My shoulders drop a fraction of an inch. 

My hands ache. My knuckles are cramped from gripping his shirt so tightly. I force my fingers to loosen, just enough to press my palms flat against his chest instead. 

Cal rests his cheek against the top of my head. He is shaking. I didn't realize that until now. A fine, steady tremor running through his arms and his chest. 

He is so scared for me. He is so relieved. 

"I'm okay," I say. 

It comes out as a rasp. It barely makes it past my teeth. 

"I know," he whispers. He doesn't move. He doesn't change the pressure of his hands. "Take your time."

I let my weight sink. I stop holding my own skeleton rigid. I let gravity work, and I let him hold me. 

The dorm is still just a dorm. The letter is still in the drawer.
