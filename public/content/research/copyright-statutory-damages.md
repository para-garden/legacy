## The Case

Jammie Thomas-Rasset, a natural resources coordinator for the Mille Lacs Band of Ojibwe in Brainerd, Minnesota — Native American, mother of four, born 1977 — used Kazaa under the username "TEREASTARR" to share music. On February 21, 2005, her IP address was logged offering 1,702 tracks. The labels sued over 24 of them: Aerosmith, Green Day, Guns N' Roses.

She had legally purchased over [200 CDs](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/news/2009/06/sony-lawyer-150k-damages-per-song-certainly-appropriate.ars), including many of the songs at issue. She was the labels' best customer. Her own rips were in WMA format. The songs in the shared folder were MP3 — tagged with credits to uploaders and ripping groups, indicating they were downloaded, not ripped from her collection.

Before filing suit, Capitol Records offered to settle for $5,000. She declined.

The final judgment: $222,000. For 24 songs. To four major record labels. By a woman who declared bankruptcy in 2013 to avoid paying it. As of 2016, [no payments had been received](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitol_Records,_Inc._v._Thomas-Rasset#Aftermath).

## The Damages Sequence

The same infringement produced three different jury verdicts:

| Trial | Year | Per song | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| First | 2007 | $9,250 | $222,000 |
| Second (retrial) | 2009 | $80,000 | $1,920,000 |
| Third | 2010 | $62,500 | $1,500,000 |

The first jury [deliberated for five minutes](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2007/10/riaa-trial-verdict-is-in-jury-finds-thomas-liable-for-infringement/). Juror Michael Hegg: ["She's a liar."](https://www.wired.com/2007/10/riaa-juror-we-w/) Another: ["We wanted to send a message."](https://www.wired.com/2007/10/riaa-juror-we-w/)

After the second trial, Judge Davis reduced the award to $54,000 — calling the original figure ["monstrous and shocking."](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/news/2010/01/judge-slashes-monstrous-p2p-award-by-97-to-54000.ars) The labels [rejected his reduction](https://web.archive.org/web/20101201000000*/cnet.com/news/jammie-thomas-rejects-riaas-25000-settlement-offer) and demanded another trial.

After the third trial, Davis again reduced to $54,000, finding the $1.5 million award "so severe and oppressive as to be wholly disproportioned to the offense and obviously unreasonable."

The Eighth Circuit reinstated the first verdict: $222,000. The Supreme Court [denied certiorari](https://www.wired.com/2013/03/supreme-court-oks-222k-verdict-for-sharing-24-songs/) on March 18, 2013.

## The Trial

Thomas-Rasset denied being the person behind the "tereastarr" account. Her lawyer suggested the computer could have been compromised — ["a spoof, a zombie or some other type of hack."](https://www.wired.com/2007/10/defense-plantin/) On the stand, she speculated that perhaps her children or then-boyfriend had installed Kazaa without her knowledge. She [cried during testimony](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/news/2009/06/thomas-testimony-ends-with-tears-anger-swedish-death-metal.ars).

No hard drive containing the copyrighted songs was ever presented at trial. Thomas-Rasset turned over a hard drive that referenced neither Kazaa nor the infringing files.

The defense's closing argument: none of the evidence pointed to Thomas-Rasset personally. It pointed to an IP address.

The plaintiffs told the jury that 1,700-plus songs were in the shared folder, that the infringement was "substantial" and "massive," that ["a message must be sent."](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/news/2009/06/sony-lawyer-150k-damages-per-song-certainly-appropriate.ars) At one point, the plaintiffs drew a [rebuke from the court](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/news/2009/06/riaa-lawyers-toss-a-skunk-in-the-jury-box-apologize.ars) for presenting new evidence of questionable relevance.

A fair use defense was [disallowed on procedural grounds](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/news/2009/06/thomas-judge-bars-fair-use-defense-oks-mediasentry-evidence.ars) — it should have been raised before the first trial.

## The Evidence

The labels' evidence came from [MediaSentry](https://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/news/2009/06/thomas-judge-bars-fair-use-defense-oks-mediasentry-evidence.ars), a company that monitored peer-to-peer networks. Thomas-Rasset's lawyers argued MediaSentry was not a licensed private investigator under the Minnesota Private Detectives Act and had violated federal wiretapping laws. The court rejected this: the MPDA did not apply to an out-of-state entity, and "there is no expectation of solitude or seclusion when a person activates a file sharing program."

In the first trial, the jury was instructed that merely "making available" sufficed to constitute infringement — no proof of actual distribution required. The judge later vacated his own verdict, calling this a possible ["manifest error of law."](https://web.archive.org/web/20120625000000*/May+15+2008+Order+indicating+possible+manifest+error) The instruction had relied on a case that was subsequently vacated by the court that issued it — without Judge Davis's knowledge.

## The Legal Mechanics

The Copyright Act (17 U.S.C. § 504(c)) allows statutory damages of $750–$30,000 per work, up to $150,000 for willful infringement. The plaintiff does not need to prove actual harm. The plaintiff does not need to prove the defendant caused them any specific loss. The plaintiff needs to prove ownership and infringement.

The constitutional threshold, under *St. Louis, I.M. & S. Railway Co. v. Williams* (1917), is whether the award is "so severe and oppressive as to be wholly disproportioned to the offense and obviously unreasonable." The Eighth Circuit found $222,000 for 24 songs — against a noncommercial individual — did not meet that threshold.

The court explicitly held that statutory damages "need not correlate to actual harm." The labels had not proven, and were not required to prove, that Thomas-Rasset's sharing of these 24 songs cost them any measurable amount.

Thomas-Rasset's attorneys argued after the third trial that the Due Process Clause had been violated because the plaintiffs hadn't proven that the defendant, specifically, had caused them any actual harm — only that file sharing, in general, had. The court agreed the award was disproportionate. The Eighth Circuit reversed.

Actual damages in a case like this are essentially zero. One person's shared folder does not move the market. The statutory regime was designed so they didn't have to prove otherwise.

## What the Labels Asked For at the End

After the Supreme Court declined to hear the case, the RIAA approached Thomas-Rasset with a proposal: they would accept a lower payment if she would [make a video about copyright infringement](https://www.wired.com/threatlevel/2013/07/riaa-asks-infamous-file-sharer/).

She refused.

## The Mechanism

**Statutory damages as amplifier.** The gap between actual harm and legal remedy in this case is not a flaw in the system — it is the system. Statutory damages exist precisely because actual damages in copyright cases are often small and difficult to prove. The legislature set the range; the jury set the number within it; the appellate court confirmed the number was constitutional. Everything worked correctly.

The multiplier here: the labels sought, and ultimately received, a judgment roughly 4,400 times what they offered to settle for. The offer was $5,000. The judgment was $222,000. The highest jury verdict was $1,920,000.

**Five minutes.** The first jury deliberated for five minutes. They awarded $222,000. That number survived six years, three trials, two reductions by the judge, and an appeal to the Supreme Court.

**Who gets made an example.** Thomas-Rasset was not a commercial pirate. She was not running a business. She was a government employee in rural Minnesota who had downloaded music — and who had legally purchased over 200 CDs from the companies suing her. The RIAA's litigation strategy in this era targeted individuals specifically — to create deterrence through visible punishment of ordinary people. The jury was told "a message must be sent."

**The bankruptcy endpoint.** The judgment is uncollectable. Thomas-Rasset declared bankruptcy in 2013. The labels received nothing. The legal system spent six years and three trials to produce a $222,000 judgment against someone who cannot pay it, as a lesson to people who would never hear about it.

**The Williams standard.** The constitutional backstop for statutory damages is a 1917 railroad case. The court that set the standard was not thinking about peer-to-peer file sharing, digital reproduction, or the difference between a commercial distributor and a person with a shared folder. The standard has not been updated. It does not ask whether the award is proportionate to harm — only whether it is "obviously unreasonable."

$222,000 for 24 songs, from a person with no commercial interest and zero measurable harm caused, was not obviously unreasonable. That is what the law currently means.
