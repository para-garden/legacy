*Nicole Dawson's emergency appendectomy was performed at Ascend Regional Medical Center, an in-network facility under her Aetna plan. [Under the No Surprises Act (effective January 1, 2022)](https://www.cms.gov/nosurprises), she should owe only in-network cost-sharing for this anesthesia service — [the Act prohibits out-of-network providers from balance-billing patients for emergency services and for non-emergency services at in-network facilities when the patient did not choose the provider.](https://www.cms.gov/nosurprises/policies-and-resources/overview-of-rules-fact-sheets) Nicole did not choose Pinnacle Anesthesia Partners. She was unconscious.*

*This bill should not exist. [The No Surprises Act requires that the patient's cost-sharing be based on the Qualifying Payment Amount (QPA) — typically the median in-network rate — not the out-of-network rate.](https://www.cms.gov/nosurprises/policies-and-resources/overview-of-rules-fact-sheets) Under the Act, Nicole's responsibility for anesthesia should be approximately $640–$900 (in-network coinsurance on the QPA), not $6,316. The $5,460 balance bill is prohibited by federal law.*

*[The bill was sent anyway.](https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/no-surprises-act-balance-billing-enforcement/) [A 2023 KFF survey found that 1 in 4 insured adults who received care in the prior year reported receiving a medical bill or EOB that contained a higher-than-expected charge, and many did not know they could dispute it.](https://www.kff.org/health-costs/issue-brief/americans-challenges-with-health-care-costs/) [CMS received over 10,000 complaints about potential No Surprises Act violations in the Act's first two years, but enforcement actions have been limited.](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-24-106335) Providers who violate the Act face penalties of up to $10,000 per violation. [In practice, enforcement relies on patient complaints — the patient must know the law exists, recognize the violation, and file a complaint.](https://www.cms.gov/nosurprises/consumers)*

*[Anesthesiologists, radiologists, pathologists, and emergency physicians are the specialties most frequently involved in out-of-network billing](https://www.healthsystemtracker.org/brief/an-examination-of-surprise-medical-bills-and-proposals-to-protect-consumers/) because they are assigned by the facility, not selected by the patient. [Before the No Surprises Act, 1 in 5 emergency room visits and 1 in 6 in-network hospitalizations involved at least one out-of-network charge.](https://www.kff.org/health-costs/issue-brief/surprise-medical-bills/) [Private equity-backed physician staffing firms — including Envision Healthcare (KKR) and TeamHealth (Blackstone) — were among the most aggressive practitioners of surprise billing.](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/06/08/upshot/surprise-billing-kkr-envision.html) [Envision filed for bankruptcy in 2023.](https://www.reuters.com/business/healthcare-pharmaceuticals/kkrs-envision-healthcare-files-bankruptcy-2023-05-15/)*

*If Nicole pays the bill, Pinnacle keeps $6,316 it is not legally entitled to. If she calls Pinnacle and cites the No Surprises Act, they will likely reprocess the claim. If she doesn't know the law exists — [and most patients don't](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/surveys/2023/nov/cost-not-getting-care-income-disparities-affordability-health) — she pays, or the bill goes to collections, or both. The protection is real. It requires the patient to enforce it.*
