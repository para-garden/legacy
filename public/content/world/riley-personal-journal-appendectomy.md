## February 21, 2032

Work thing finally over. The Braddock rollout that has been eating my life since November. Dana sent a 4pm all-hands to say it went live without incident and I thought, that's it, that's the whole winter, five months of my life summarized in a Slack message with a thumbs-up reaction.

Ordered Thai food. Watched two episodes of something I can't remember now. Felt vaguely glad.

Should do laundry.

---

## February 23, 2032

Something is off in my side. Not bad. More like a low-level weirdness, the way your body flags something before it becomes anything. Drank water, took some Advil, went to bed.

---

## February 24, 2032

Woke up at maybe 1am. The pain had moved and gotten worse. Lower right, specific. Not vague anymore.

I Googled it, which you're not supposed to do, and all the results said the same thing in different ways. I thought: probably not. Probably something I ate. The Thai food, maybe. I lay there for a while trying to decide if it was the Thai food.

It was not the Thai food.

By 2am I couldn't find a position. I was on the bathroom floor, which is where I ended up — not dramatically, just gradually, the floor seeming like the right place to be. I thought about calling someone to drive me. Went through the list. It was 2am. There was nobody I wanted to call at 2am and then make sit in an ER until dawn. I knew even then that the ambulance would cost more. I made that calculation consciously, from the bathroom floor, and then the pain got bad enough that I stopped calculating and called 911.

They were there in about eight minutes. That part I remember clearly — counting the seconds, something to do.

The rest of February 24th is approximate. CT scan. Blood draw. A lot of waiting. A nurse named Demetria who had an extremely calm voice and explained everything twice without making me feel bad for needing it explained twice. The surgeon came in and said the word *appendectomy* and it landed sort of flatly, like: oh, so that's the thing that's happening.

They took me back around 11:30. The anesthesiologist introduced himself. I don't remember his name.

---

## February 26, 2032

Home. They kept me two nights. The second day I was mostly just waiting to be discharged, watching a TV mounted too high on the wall, eating a turkey sandwich off a tray that arrived at 11:30am with no warning.

My neighbor Priya let me in because I'd given her a spare key for emergencies and this counted. She'd put some groceries in my fridge without being asked. Soup, yogurt, bananas, the kind of thing you can eat when you don't feel like eating. I cried a little after she left, which surprised me.

The apartment looked exactly the same. Which is obvious but somehow notable — nothing knew anything had happened.

I'm on the couch. Can't sit all the way up yet. They gave me three small incisions and the middle one complains when I twist. Instructions say no lifting over ten pounds for two weeks. I don't currently want to lift anything.

Taking the Tylenol as scheduled. Trying to remember to do the breathing thing with the spirometer they sent me home with, which feels absurd, breathing into a plastic tube, but apparently your lungs do something bad if you don't.

---

## February 28, 2032

Still on the couch. Working from home — Dana said I could take the week but I said I was fine, which was mostly true. I had the laptop on a pillow and joined the Thursday standup, which took eleven minutes and could have been an email. Normal.

The incision in the middle is itching now, which they said means it's healing. I keep resisting touching it.

I have not looked at my health insurance portal yet.

---

## March 4, 2032

Two envelopes came today. I looked at them on the table for about twenty minutes before I opened them.

The first one is the hospital bill. Total charges $54,817. My portion: $4,247. There's a payment plan option — interest-free, up to 24 months. I can do $177 a month. That's the part that is fine.

The part that is not fine is the sheet inside that says I'll be getting separate bills from the surgeon, the anesthesiologist, the radiologist, and the pathologist. All different companies. They might not all be in-network. Particularly the anesthesiologist — there's a full paragraph about it. *Pinnacle Anesthesia Partners is not a participating provider with all insurance plans.* He was just there. I did not select him.

The second envelope is from BlueCross. Not a bill. Just an Explanation of Benefits.

The header of the EOB, in large text, says: YOUR SAVINGS THIS CLAIM: $36,204.00

Because I chose an in-network provider.

I texted Jamie.

---

## March 7, 2032

Went back into the office today. First time since the 24th. Nothing had changed. My desk was exactly the same. Someone had put a plant near the windows and I didn't know whose it was. I got a coffee and answered emails for four hours and went home.

Demetria, the nurse, said it would take three to four weeks to feel normal. I think that's right. There's a tiredness that isn't like regular tired — it's more like my body is just running a quiet background process that's using up resources.

---

## March 12, 2032

Another bill. This one's from the surgeon — a different company entirely, not Ascend, a group practice called Southeast Surgical Associates. $1,847 billed. Insurance paid $1,240. My portion: $607. Separate payment portal, different phone number, a new account number to track.

I am now keeping a spreadsheet. This feels like something I should have set up earlier.

Current known balances:
- Ascend Health System: $4,247
- Southeast Surgical Associates: $607
- Ambulance: unknown (need to file form)
- Anesthesiologist: unknown
- Radiologist: unknown
- Pathologist: unknown

Column headers: creditor, amount, due date, status, payment plan Y/N, notes. I put *filed ambulance form* in the notes column as a reminder. I have not filed it yet.

---

## March 19, 2032

Pathologist bill: $312. Radiologist bill: $448. Both in-network. Both smaller than I feared.

Started the 24-month plan with Ascend. $177 starting April 1st. Set up autopay.

I still need to find the ambulance form.

---

## March 27, 2032

Friday. Made dinner with Priya — she wanted to do a real thank-you for the groceries and I said that was completely unnecessary and she came over anyway and made pasta. We talked for three hours. I did not think about the spreadsheet once.

The anesthesiologist bill came yesterday. Pinnacle Anesthesia Partners. I opened it before I let myself think about it. In-network. I don't know why or how — maybe because the hospital was in-network — but the determination came back in-network. My portion: $382.

I updated the spreadsheet.

Total out of pocket, known items: $5,996. Plus the ambulance, which I need to file. Plus whatever the ambulance decision comes back as.

That's the number for the thing that happened to me on February 24th.

---

## April 8, 2032

Feels like spring finally. I walked to work twice this week, which I hadn't done since fall. The incisions are gone — not even visible really, just three faint marks if you know where to look.

I found the METS form. It was exactly where I'd written it down in the spreadsheet notes — a PDF from the METS billing page, their specific form, eight pages. I downloaded it and got as far as filling in my name and address and the date of service and then I had to find my insurance card information and I got up to do something else and didn't come back to it.

I have until May 24th.

---

## April 22, 2032

Nothing to report really. Work is fine. Project with the Heller account is going okay. I went to a birthday thing for Marcus on Saturday and ate too much cake and it was nice.

---

## May 3, 2032

The form. I need to do the form.

---

## May 19, 2032

I sat down to do the form tonight. Found the PDF. Five days left.

I got through all of it — the service date, the transport origin and destination, my policy number, the group number, the claim number from the BlueCross EOB for the hospital visit, or the closest thing to it. The instructions say to attach a copy of the METS itemized bill, which I had not received, and the EOB, and a completed W-9, and a signed statement authorizing release of medical records.

I don't have the itemized bill from METS. I never got one. I had the number from the Ascend bill — *estimated ambulance charge: $2,800–$4,200* — but that's not the same thing. I called the number on the Ascend bill, (833) 620-4411, and got an automated system that said their office hours were Monday through Friday 8am to 6pm.

It's Sunday.

I put the form in a folder.

---

## May 25, 2032

The window was May 24th.

I didn't call them back Monday. I don't know why exactly — I meant to and then didn't, and then a few days went by, and then the 24th was yesterday.

It's gone. Whatever reimbursement I might have gotten. I don't know the exact number — somewhere in the $2,800 to $4,200 range is what they estimated, but I never got an actual bill. Now I'll get one.

I'm not going to do anything about this tonight. I'm going to finish my tea and go to bed.

---

## June 14, 2032

I keep thinking the spreadsheet is done and then remembering it isn't.

Everything else is on autopay or paid. The Ascend plan is running. The surgeon was paid. The anesthesiologist, the radiologist, the pathologist — all paid. The known stuff is handled.

The ambulance is still a cell that says *unknown — form not filed.*

I should change that. Something like *outstanding — form window closed* or something. Just so the cell is accurate.

I haven't changed it.

---

## July 4, 2032

Holiday. Went to Marcus and Yolanda's for a cookout. Their yard is small but they string lights in the tree and it's enough. Came home late, full, a little sunburned on the back of my neck.

I am okay.

---

## August 3, 2032

Six months since the appendix.

I'm not sure why I noticed that. It came up because I was scheduling my follow-up with my primary care and the intake form asked if I'd had any hospitalizations in the past year and I had to think for a second, the way you think about something that happened to someone who used to be you.

I checked and the deductible and the out-of-pocket max both reset in January. So February 24th hit in a fresh year. That's why I owe what I owe. If it had been November, December, I would have been close to my max already.

Anyway. I was fine before and I am fine now.

---

## September 12, 2032

Got a letter yesterday. From a company called Resolute Recovery Solutions. I looked at the return address and knew what it was before I opened it.

METS. The ambulance. They got a bill, apparently — $3,180 original, adjusted to $2,847 after whatever adjustment they applied. Gone to collections September 11th.

It came with a lot of text about my rights under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act. Thirty days to dispute. I can pay online. I can call. I can write a letter to a P.O. Box.

I read the whole thing. Set it on the table next to my keys.

I'm going to pay it. Not today. I don't have $2,847 sitting around, so I'll have to call and do a payment plan, the way you do. I'll do it this week, probably.

The letter says if I pay it will still be on my credit report for however many years — "may affect your credit score" — but that it will be updated to reflect that it's resolved. I don't know what that means for the number. I should find out.

$177 a month to Ascend through January 2034. And now this.

The appendix tried to kill me in February. It's September and I'm still paying for it, in small amounts, to several different companies, two of whom I had never heard of before February 24th and one of whom I knew nothing about until yesterday.

That's the whole thing. I don't know what else I expected.

---

## September 18, 2032

Called Resolute this morning before work. Forty-minute hold. Set up a payment plan — $160 a month for eighteen months. They emailed a confirmation with a new account number.

I updated the spreadsheet. Closed the cell that said *unknown — form not filed.* Put in $2,847 and the payment plan start date.

Priya knocked on my door in the evening to return a book she'd borrowed. We talked in the hallway for a while. She's starting a new job next month, the one she'd been hoping for. I told her that was great and meant it.

Walked back into my apartment. The spreadsheet was still open on my laptop.

I'm going to be fine.

I just need to keep track of everything.
