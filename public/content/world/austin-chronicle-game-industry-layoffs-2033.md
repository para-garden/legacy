## 'Nobody Saw This Coming' — Austin Game Studios Hit by Wave of Layoffs

**Three studios, roughly 400 jobs, in the span of six weeks**

*By Elaine Marchetti*

The Austin game development community is reeling from a string of studio closures and layoffs that have eliminated roughly 400 jobs since late March, adding to a [national wave of industry cuts that has displaced more than 16,000 game workers since January](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/game-industry-layoffs-in-2024).

Arclight Studios, a subsidiary of publisher Voltan Entertainment, [confirmed in late April](https://www.ign.com/) that it had laid off approximately 40% of its staff — around 48 employees — three months after shipping the critically acclaimed open-world title *Heliograph*. Arclight's Studio Director David Moreno posted on LinkedIn that the reductions were "the hardest decision of my career" and offered to serve as a personal reference for affected workers.

Earlier in April, Starpoint Interactive shuttered its Austin office entirely, affecting 140 employees. The closure came eight months after its parent company, Axis Media Group, acquired Starpoint in what was described at the time as a "commitment to the Austin creative ecosystem."

And in late March, indie studio Hollowpoint Games laid off its entire QA department — 22 people — while maintaining its core development team of 40. Hollowpoint co-founder Amy Chen said in a statement that the studio was "restructuring to meet the realities of the current funding environment."

Together, the three rounds of cuts represent the worst stretch for Austin's game development sector since the [industry-wide contraction of 2023–2024](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/game-industry-layoffs-in-2024).

---

### 'We shipped the game. Then we shipped ourselves.'

For workers at Arclight, the timing was particularly bitter. *Heliograph* launched in January to strong reviews — [an 86 on Metacritic](https://www.metacritic.com/) and first-month sales that exceeded Voltan's projections. Several affected employees told the *Chronicle* they had worked extended hours for months leading up to ship, including evenings and weekends.

"We crunched for four months straight," said one former environment artist who requested anonymity. "They told us the milestone bonus was our reward for that sacrifice. I got the bonus in February and my termination email in April."

The former employee said affected workers were given 30 minutes to collect their belongings and that building access was revoked by the time some returned from clearing their desks. Arclight disputes this characterization, saying in a statement that "all affected employees were treated with dignity and respect throughout the separation process."

Severance for Arclight employees was two weeks of base pay per year of service, with a two-week minimum. For recently hired workers, that amounted to two weeks. [The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that only 34% of private-sector workers have access to severance pay at all](https://www.bls.gov/ncs/).

---

### The cost of the cycle

Austin has positioned itself as a game development hub for over a decade, with tax incentives, a deep talent pool from UT's game design program, and a cost of living — though rapidly rising — that remains below the Bay Area. The [Austin Chamber of Commerce estimated in 2031](https://www.austinchamber.com/) that the game industry contributed $1.2 billion annually to the local economy and supported roughly 4,800 direct jobs.

But those numbers obscure a pattern that workers say is endemic. Studios ramp up for production, hire aggressively, ship, and then cut — a cycle that leaves workers in a permanent state of insecurity. [The IGDA's most recent Developer Satisfaction Survey](https://igda.org/dss/) found that 36% of respondents had been laid off at least once, and 10% had been laid off three or more times.

"Nobody saw this coming" is what people keep saying, and it's just not true," said Chris Barlow, a veteran technical artist who has survived four rounds of layoffs across three studios over 12 years. "Everyone saw this coming. We just didn't want to say it out loud during crunch because then you're the negative person and the negative person gets cut first."

---

### Health coverage and the 'COBRA gap'

For laid-off workers, one of the most immediate concerns is health insurance. Under [COBRA](https://www.dol.gov/general/topic/health-plans/cobra), terminated employees can continue their employer-sponsored coverage for up to 18 months — but at the full premium cost, with no employer subsidy. Former Arclight employees say their COBRA quotes ranged from $580 to $1,200 per month, depending on plan tier and family size.

"I was paying $120 a month for my health insurance while I was employed," said one former QA tester. "The COBRA letter said it would be $940. For the same coverage. I can't afford that on two weeks of severance."

The [ACA marketplace](https://www.healthcare.gov/) open enrollment period ended January 15, and special enrollment triggered by job loss requires application within 60 days. Several workers said they were unsure of the timeline or process.

---

### What's next

A [Game Workers Alliance Austin](https://www.gameworkersalliance.org/) chapter meeting held last Thursday at a South Congress coffee shop drew more than 60 attendees, most of them recently laid off. Organizers said it was the largest turnout they had seen.

"I think people are done pretending this is normal," said organizer Dani Reeves, a former narrative designer. "We ship billion-dollar products and then we're surprised when the people who made them can't afford rent?"

Job boards for the Austin game community have seen a surge in applicants. One popular Discord server for Austin game dev job seekers now has over 2,300 members, up from 800 a year ago.

For the former Arclight environment artist, the plan is simpler. "I'm going home to Kentucky for a while. My mom has a room. I'll figure it out from there."
