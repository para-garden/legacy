### What you need to know

1. **Make all payments on time.** If you miss a payment or pay late, we may terminate this agreement and take [collection action](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/collection-process-for-taxpayers-filing-and-or-paying-late), including filing a federal tax lien or issuing a levy on your wages, bank accounts, or other property.

2. **File all future tax returns on time.** If you fail to file a return or pay taxes owed for any future tax year, this agreement will default.

3. **[Estimated tax payments](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/estimated-taxes).** As a self-employed individual, you are required to make quarterly estimated tax payments for the current tax year (2031). Payment vouchers (Form 1040-ES) are available at irs.gov/payments. Failure to make estimated payments may result in additional penalties.

4. **This agreement does not reduce the amount you owe.** You are paying the full amount plus interest and any applicable penalties.

### Understanding your self-employment tax

As an [independent contractor](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/independent-contractor-self-employed-or-employee), you are responsible for paying [self-employment tax](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/self-employment-tax-social-security-and-medicare-taxes), which covers your Social Security and Medicare contributions. Unlike traditional employees, whose employers pay half of these taxes, self-employed individuals pay the full [15.3%](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/self-employment-tax-social-security-and-medicare-taxes) (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) on net self-employment income.

For tax year 2030, your net self-employment income was approximately $15,800, resulting in a self-employment tax liability of $2,418.
