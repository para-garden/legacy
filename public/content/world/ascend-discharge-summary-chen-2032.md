### History of Present Illness

Mr. Chen is a 34-year-old male with no significant past medical history who presented to the Ascend Regional Medical Center Emergency Department on June 18, 2032, at 02:14 AM with complaints of severe abdominal pain, fever, and vomiting.

Patient reports that symptoms began approximately **72 hours prior to presentation** with periumbilical pain that migrated to the right lower quadrant over the first 12–18 hours. He describes initial pain as moderate, rated 5/10, which progressively worsened to 9/10 at time of presentation. He reports nausea, vomiting (×4 over the preceding 24 hours), and subjective fever. He took ibuprofen 600mg approximately every 6 hours starting on day 1 of symptoms.

**Patient states he delayed presenting to the emergency department due to concerns about cost.** He reports being uninsured and not having a primary care physician. He describes presenting only when pain became "unbearable" and he was unable to stand upright. Patient arrived via personal vehicle (companion drove); EMS not utilized.

---

### Emergency Department Course

On arrival: T 39.2°C (102.6°F), HR 118, BP 102/64, RR 22, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Patient appeared acutely ill, diaphoretic, lying in the fetal position. Abdomen rigid with diffuse tenderness, most pronounced RLQ. Positive rebound, positive Rovsing's. Bowel sounds absent.

CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast demonstrated:
- Perforated appendix with adjacent phlegmon
- Free fluid in the pelvis and right paracolic gutter
- Gas tracking along the right psoas, consistent with perforation
- 3.8 cm pelvic fluid collection suggesting early abscess formation

Labs notable for: WBC 21.4 (left shift with 18% bands), lactate 2.8, CRP 284. Mild AKI with creatinine 1.4 (baseline unknown, presumed normal). Blood cultures obtained ×2.

Assessment: **Perforated appendicitis with diffuse peritonitis and early sepsis.** Surgical consultation obtained emergently.

---

### Operative Report Summary

**Date:** June 18, 2032
**Start Time:** 04:48 AM
**Procedure:** Exploratory laparotomy, appendectomy, peritoneal lavage, drain placement

Laparoscopic approach was attempted initially but converted to open within approximately 15 minutes due to extensive adhesions, purulent peritoneal fluid, and inability to safely identify the appendiceal stump laparoscopically.

**Findings:** Gangrenous, perforated appendix at the tip with fecalith. Approximately 400 mL of frank pus in the pelvis and right lower quadrant. Pelvic abscess cavity approximately 4 cm, partially walled off. Small bowel loops matted with fibrinous exudate.

Appendix removed. Peritoneal cavity irrigated with 6 liters warm saline. Jackson-Pratt drain placed in the pelvis. Fascia closed with running #1 PDS. Skin left open for delayed primary closure due to contamination.

EBL: 150 mL. No complications. Patient transferred to SICU post-operatively.

### Hospital Course

**POD 0–2 (SICU):** Patient admitted to the surgical intensive care unit for hemodynamic monitoring and sepsis management. Started on piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q8h. Required 3L crystalloid resuscitation in the first 12 hours. Lactate normalized by POD 1. Blood cultures returned positive for *E. coli* on POD 2; sensitivities confirmed susceptibility to current antibiotic regimen. Remained NPO with nasogastric decompression. SICU stay: 2 days.

**POD 2–4 (Floor):** Transferred to surgical floor. JP drain output initially 80–120 mL/day of serosanguinous fluid. Repeat CT on POD 3 demonstrated interval decrease in pelvic fluid collection but a new 2.4 cm loculated collection posterior to the cecum. Pain managed with IV acetaminophen and ketorolac; opioids minimized per enhanced recovery protocol. Diet advanced slowly — clear liquids POD 2, regular diet POD 4 with poor tolerance.

**POD 4 (Percutaneous Drainage):** Interventional radiology placed a CT-guided percutaneous drain into the posterior cecal collection. Aspirate sent for culture (grew *E. coli* and *Bacteroides fragilis*). Antibiotic regimen broadened to meropenem 1g IV q8h. Drain left to gravity.

**POD 5–6:** Clinical improvement. Afebrile for 48 hours. WBC trending down (14.2 → 9.8). Drain output decreasing. Tolerating regular diet. Ambulating with assistance. Surgical wound assessed for delayed primary closure — wound edges well-approximated, no signs of infection.

**POD 7 (Discharge):** Drain output <15 mL/day. Labs stable. Patient ambulating independently. Wound partially closed with Steri-Strips; packing continued for the open segment. Percutaneous drain capped; will be assessed for removal at follow-up.

*An [uncomplicated laparoscopic appendectomy](https://ascend-hospital-bill-appendectomy) — the surgery Riley Whitaker had at this same hospital — requires a 1–2 day hospital stay. A [perforated appendectomy with peritonitis](https://hcup-us.ahrq.gov/reports/statbriefs/sb159.jsp) requires an average of 5.2 days. Mr. Chen's stay was 7 days, which included 2 days in the ICU and a return to interventional radiology for a second drainage procedure.*

*[The risk of appendiceal perforation is minimal in the first 36 hours of symptoms. After 36 hours, the risk increases approximately 5% for every 12-hour period of delay.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16500243/) Mr. Chen presented at approximately 72 hours — well past the threshold. [Mean hospital cost for perforated appendicitis is $12,800 compared to $7,800 for uncomplicated cases](https://hcup-us.ahrq.gov/reports/statbriefs/sb159.jsp) (these are hospital costs, not what patients are billed). On the chargemaster, the difference is larger: [the uncomplicated appendectomy at Ascend was billed at $54,817](https://ascend-hospital-bill-appendectomy); Mr. Chen's charges — open surgery, ICU, drain placement, 7-day stay, second procedure — will exceed $100,000.*

*[36% of U.S. adults report skipping or postponing needed medical care because of cost.](https://www.kff.org/health-costs/americans-challenges-with-health-care-costs/) [In 2022, 38% of Americans — an estimated 98 million people — put off medical treatment due to cost, a record high.](https://news.gallup.com/poll/468053/record-high-put-off-medical-care-due-cost-2022.aspx) [The United States has the highest rate of cost-related access problems among all high-income countries.](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/fund-reports/2024/sep/mirror-mirror-2024) The next highest is Switzerland, at roughly half the U.S. rate.*

*[In 2017, a 26-year-old named Alec Smith aged off his mother's insurance, could not afford his insulin at $1,300 per month, rationed it, and died of diabetic ketoacidosis.](https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2018/09/01/641615877/insulins-high-cost-leads-to-lethal-rationing) He died three days before payday. The condition that killed him is treatable with a medication that costs [$2–4 to manufacture per vial](https://gh.bmj.com/content/3/5/e000850). Mr. Chen delayed 72 hours. His appendix perforated. The delay turned a routine surgery into a complicated one. [Mortality for non-perforated appendicitis is less than 0.1%. For perforated appendicitis, it is 1–5%.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1876946/) Mr. Chen survived.*

*The financial counseling note records that Mr. Chen cannot complete his financial assistance application because he has not filed his 2031 tax return. [Only 29% of patients eligible for hospital financial assistance actually receive it.](https://dollarfor.org/the-path-to-charity-care/) The application requires eight categories of documentation submitted within 240 days. He has approximately 170 days remaining. He works for himself. He needs to file a tax return he hasn't filed in order to apply for assistance on a bill he received because he delayed care he couldn't afford.*
