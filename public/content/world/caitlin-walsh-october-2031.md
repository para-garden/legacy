The phone buzzes against my mattress. Then again. Then three times in rapid succession.

It's the main group chat. *Where did u guys go?* *Did Jade leave with that guy from Pike?* *Someone let me in the downstairs door I forgot my swipe.*

Usually, I'd be the first to type back. I'd be the one sending a blurry picture of Jade eating pizza on the sidewalk. I'd be in it.

I'm just watching the screen light up the dark ceiling of our dorm room.

Jade is asleep across the room. She came in about an hour after I did, dropping her keys on the linoleum, giggling at something on her phone. She whispered, *"Caity? You awake?"* and I kept my eyes squeezed shut. I made my breathing deep and even, the way you do when you're pretending. She turned off the overhead light, rustled around in her makeup bag, and passed out in her clothes. I'm in my clothes, too. The jeans I borrowed from her. The black tank top.

I feel incredibly still. Like if I move, the room is going to shatter.

I am a Communications major. I take classes on interpersonal dynamics and media messaging. I know the words for things. But I don't know what to call the last three hours.

It was just a normal Friday. We got ready together while listening to that playlist Jade made. We walked to Lambda Sigma. The air was cold — that sharp, late-October cold where you shiver in your going-out top but you don't care because you're with your people. We had drinks in the basement. I laughed at a joke. I laugh easily.

Then I was upstairs. I don't even remember the exact transition. It was just flirting, it was just the normal script of a Friday night, until the door clicked shut and the script changed.

I said no.

I said it. I know I said it. I said, *"Wait, no."* I said, *"I don't want to."*

I thought that was how it worked. You say no, and the other person goes, *Oh, my bad,* and things get a little awkward, and then you go back downstairs and find your friends. That's the rule. But he just kept going. He just shifted his weight, and pinned my arm, and the music from the basement was thumping through the floorboards — this heavy, vibrating bass — and he didn't stop.

My chest feels tight. It feels like someone poured cement down my throat.

The phone buzzes again. It's my sister, Erin.
*Look at this dog,* the text says, with a TikTok link.

I stare at Erin's name. I usually text Erin when something is funny. I open the message thread. I watch the little cursor blink.

*Erin, I think something bad happened to me.*

I don't type it. I close out of the app.

My life is good. My parents are good, they're asleep in the house I grew up in. My roommate is wonderful, I got so lucky with the draw. I have class on Tuesday. I go to the dining hall in the morning and I get an iced coffee with oat milk and two pumps of vanilla. I get the exact same thing every time. I like my life. I'm just in it, and it's enough. It's always been enough.

Why did he do that?

I sit up slowly. My body hurts. There is a dull, heavy ache, a soreness in my wrists. I look down at my hands in the pale light coming through the blinds. They don't look like my hands. I feel like I am floating somewhere near the ceiling, looking down at a twenty-year-old girl sitting on a twin bed in the dark.

Jade sighs in her sleep and rolls over. Her blanket slips off her shoulder.

I want to wake her up. I want to say, *Jade, please look at me.* But if I wake her up, I have to say it out loud. And if I say it out loud, then it's real. Then I'm not Caitlin who laughs easily and shows up when people make plans. I'm a girl who went upstairs and didn't come back down the same.

I pull my knees up to my chest. I wrap my arms around my legs, making myself as small as possible.

I open Instagram. I tap the Reels tab. The algorithm doesn't know what happened in the Lambda house. The algorithm thinks I want to see makeup tutorials, and golden retrievers, and girls doing dorm-room cooking hacks. I scroll. The audio is muted, so it's just moving pictures. Bright colors. Smiling faces.

I scroll.

I scroll.

I just want it to be yesterday. I just want it to be yesterday so bad.

The sky outside the window turns a bruised, terrible purple, and then a weak gray. Morning. It's Saturday morning.

My phone battery drops to 9%. I finally lock the screen and put it face-down on the nightstand.

I grab my shower caddy and a towel. The hallway is dead quiet. It's 6:30 AM on a Saturday. Everyone is sleeping it off. The fluorescent lights in the communal bathroom buzz overhead. I lock the door to the big stall at the end and sit down.

When I pull down Jade's jeans, I see a faint, purplish bruise forming high on the inside of my left thigh. Shaped like the pad of a thumb.

I stare at it. A wave of nausea hits me so fast and so hard that I have to lean forward, resting my forehead against the cold metal door of the stall. I dry heave, but nothing comes up. Just a sour burn in my throat.

I move to the showers. I turn the water as hot as it will go. I step in still wearing my underwear, peeling them off only when they're soaked through.

I am not crying. I'm just methodically scrubbing my skin with my peach-scented body wash. I am washing my arms, my chest, between my legs, rinsing and repeating. I just feel incredibly, overwhelmingly tired.

---

When I get back to the room, Jade is shifting.

"Ugh," she groans. "Kill me."

"Hey," I say. My voice sounds normal. A little scratchy, but normal.

"What time is it?"

"A little before seven."

"Why are you up? Why are you clean?" She rubs her face. "Did I leave you last night? I think I lost you after they brought out the tequila."

This is the moment. The space between her question and my answer.

I could say it. Jade would catch me. She's a good person. She would sit up, she would be horrified, she would hug me, she would make phone calls.

"I just got tired," I say. I turn toward the closet, pulling a clean sweatshirt off a hanger so I don't have to look at her. "I walked back. You were right behind me anyway."

"Oh. Okay." Jade sighs, closing her eyes again. "Good. I thought I abandoned you. Are we getting dining hall coffee later?"

"Yeah. Same order."

"Oat milk vanilla," Jade mumbles, already drifting back. "You're so predictable, Caity."

"I know."

I sit back down on the edge of my freshly made bed. I look at Jade's peaceful, sleeping face. I look at the posters on our walls.

I told the lie, and the room didn't shatter. Everything looks exactly the same. But I'm trapped behind a glass wall, watching my good life go on without me.

---

At 10:15 AM, I put on my Caitlin costume.

The walk to the dining hall is agonizingly normal. Groups of girls are walking in their athleisure, wearing sunglasses to hide their hangovers. Guys are throwing a football on the quad.

I keep expecting someone to stop me. I feel like there should be a siren going off, or a neon sign pointing down at my head: *Look at her. Look at what happened.*

But no one looks.

We find our usual table by the windows. Maya and Sarah are already there.

"Oh my god, look who survived," Sarah says.

Maya takes a sip of her water and turns to me. "Caity, where did you go? I swear I saw you talking to that guy — the tall one from upstairs, the one in the blue flannel — and then you vanished."

The ice in my cup rattles. My hand is shaking. I put the cup down on the table.

*The tall one from upstairs.*

Suddenly I can smell him again. Cheap cologne, stale beer, the sweat on his neck. I can feel the heavy weight of his chest pressing me into the mattress. I can feel the rough carpet against the back of my hand.

My heart starts hammering against my ribs. *I said no. I said wait.*

I look at Maya's expectant face. She's smiling. She's waiting for gossip.

"I just wasn't feeling it," I hear myself say. "I ghosted. Went home and doomscrolled for an hour."

Maya laughs. "You missed a fight out on the lawn, anyway."

And just like that, the spotlight moves off me. The conversation flows around me, a fast-moving river, and I am standing perfectly still on the bank.

I take a sip of my iced coffee. It tastes exactly the way it always tastes. Sweet, creamy, cold. I swallow it past the lump of cement in my throat.

My phone buzzes.
*Mom: Good morning! Did you girls have fun last night? Erin said you went out.*
*Mom: Call me later if you have time, dad is making chili! Love you!*

I type back: *Had fun! We just hung out. I'll call you guys after I study ❤️*

I put the phone face-down.

Maya tells a joke. Jade laughs. Sarah laughs. I smile, and then I laugh, too. I force the air up from my lungs, through my tight chest, past my bruised throat, and out into the dining hall.

I laugh easily. Everyone knows that about me.

I look around the table at my friends. My people. I love them, but looking at them right now feels like looking at them through the wrong end of a telescope.

"So, what's the move for tonight?" Sarah asks on the walk back. "Pike is having a thing."

"I think I might actually stay in," I say. I keep my tone light, almost apologetic. "I have to catch up on reading for media ethics. I'm so behind."

"Booo," Sarah says, bumping her shoulder against mine. "You'll get FOMO by ten o'clock, watch."

"Probably," I smile.

Back in our room, Jade changes into sweatpants and opens her laptop. "You watching?"

"Yeah," I say. I walk over and sit on the edge of her mattress.

On the screen, people are crying about who loves who. Fighting about text messages and miscommunications. I stare at the bright, saturated colors. It all seems so incredibly trivial, like a broadcast from a planet I don't live on anymore.

I pull my phone out. Erin's text is still sitting there from last night. *Look at this dog.*

I click the link. A golden retriever trying to carry a massive branch through a doorway that's too narrow, bumping against the frame, looking confused, trying again.

I watch it three times.

I tap the text box.

*Erin, please come get me.*
*Erin, he held me down.*
*Erin, I'm so scared.*

I delete it all.

I type: *Hahahaha.*
I type: *Me trying to get through this semester.*

I hit send.

A second later, the three little dots appear. Erin is typing.
*Right?? Miss u! Tell Jade I said hi.*

I lock the phone. The dorm room is warm now. The radiator is hissing. Jade laughs at something on the TV and nudges my shoulder with her foot.

"Can you believe him?" she asks.

"No," I say. "Crazy."

I am Caitlin Walsh. I am twenty. I am from Cincinnati. My parents are together. I have an older sister. My roommate is Jade. My life is good.

I lean back against the wall, pull my knees up to my chest, and fix my eyes on the screen. I am not building toward anything. I am just in it. I am in it, and I don't know how I am ever going to get out.
