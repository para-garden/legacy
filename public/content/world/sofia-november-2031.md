It's a window envelope. You can see the laser-printed letters right through the thin plastic film.

First line: the name.
Second line: Student ID number.
Third line: Campus mailbox.

It's just a database query. I know how it works. Someone in the Title IX office clicked a button to generate the intake notice, and the software pulled the `legal_name` field instead of `preferred_name`. It's just a line of code. It's not a person trying to hurt me. It's just a machine doing what it was built to do.

I run my thumb over the plastic window.

When I sat in that office weeks ago and told the coordinator what happened at the party, what he did, what he said to me while he did it — she called me Sofia. She handed me a paper cup of water and she called me Sofia. She wrote it down on her legal pad.

But the system doesn't read legal pads. The system talks to the registrar. The registrar talks to my birth certificate, and the state of Arizona wants a couple hundred dollars and a court order that I don't have yet to fix it.

So here we are.

He gets to be exactly who he is. The guy from my lit seminar. The guy who knew exactly what I was and decided that meant he could take whatever he wanted. The system knows his name. The system has no trouble with him.

And I get to receive mail addressed to a ghost. A formal letter sent to a phantom, asking about a violence done to me.

---

I tear the top edge. It's ragged, a clumsy rip, and I pull out the pages. Two sheets, thick university stock, stapled at the corner.

*Dear [Name].*

There it is again. Right at the top.

I scan past it. I don't have the energy to draft another polite, carefully worded email to the Title IX office reminding them to manually adjust their outgoing templates. That's a Tuesday problem. Today is just about reading the words.

*Notice of Preliminary Inquiry.*
*Alleged violation of the Student Code of Conduct, Section 4.1: Non-Consensual Sexual Contact.*
*Mutual No-Contact Directive.*

I stop at that last phrase. *Mutual.*

I trace the word with my fingernail. It's such an institutional word. It implies an equal footing. It says: *you two couldn't play nicely, so we are putting you in separate corners.* It means I am formally forbidden from approaching the guy who pinned my wrists to a mattress, just in case I was planning on, what, hunting him down between classes?

I know why they do it. It's a liability shield for the school. It's standard operating procedure. I know the mechanics of the machine. But knowing how an engine works doesn't make it any less loud when you're standing right next to it.

My phone vibrates against the desk. Maya.

*Dining hall at 6? They have those dry chicken tenders you pretend to hate.*

I look at the phone, then back at the letter.

There's a date at the bottom of the second page. They want me to come back in for a follow-up interview next Wednesday at two in the afternoon. I have my Victorian Lit seminar on Wednesdays at two. The same seminar he is in.

I stare at the scheduling conflict. It's almost funny, if you squint at it. The system is telling me I can't attend the class where the guy who assaulted me sits three rows back, because the system needs me to miss that class to talk about how he assaulted me. I'll have to email the professor. *Dear Dr. Harrison, I will be absent on Wednesday due to an administrative conflict.*

Smooth. Professional. Giving nothing away.

I fold the letter along its original creases. I open my bottom desk drawer — underneath the spare highlighters, underneath a crumpled receipt from the campus bookstore — and I slide it in. I shut the drawer.

I pick up my phone.

*See you at 6,* I type back. *Save me some honey mustard.*

---

I stand up. The radiator is clanking, a rhythmic metallic thud that means the building is trying to fight off the November freeze. I walked into the cold on purpose two years ago. I wanted a place where the air bites your face and makes you remember you're awake, a place where nobody knew the before.

I grab my heavy wool coat off the back of the chair.

There is a specific kind of tired that settles in your bones when you have to do the math for everybody else. I have to figure out how to email the lit professor without making him uncomfortable. I have to figure out how to remind the Title IX office about my name without making the coordinator feel defensive about her software. I have to figure out how to sit with Maya at dinner and be exactly the right amount of okay so she doesn't panic, but not so okay that she thinks I'm shutting her out.

It's all calibration. Dialing the knobs so the machine keeps running.

I catch my reflection in the dark glass of the window before I turn off the desk lamp. Just a shape in the glass, illuminated by the halogen streetlights outside.

I look like myself. I have looked like myself for a long time. The piece of paper in the drawer doesn't change that. The guy in the seminar doesn't change that, no matter what he thought he was doing, no matter what part of me he thought he was reducing me to.

They didn't take my name. They didn't take me. They just make it exhausting to exist.

I shove my hands into my coat pockets. I can feel the shape of my room keys. My student ID — the one with the right photo and the wrong name, the piece of plastic I have to hand to the dining hall cashier in ten minutes.

Wednesday is going to happen. I am going to sit in that sterile office and I will probably have to correct them on the name thing again and I will do it politely. I will file the paperwork in my head. I will get through the preliminary inquiry because the alternative is letting him think he broke me, or letting the university decide I'm too complicated to process.

I take a breath. It catches a little in my chest, but I push it out.

Time for dinner.
