I stand up.

I don't calculate the distance. I don't check the angle. I just walk across the rug.

Cal looks up from the floor. His eyes dart to my face, confused, and then he starts to shift, his weight dropping back, trying to do the math to get out of my way before I cross his perimeter.

I don't give him the time.

I step between his knees. I sit down on his lap. I wrap my arms around his neck, press my face into his shoulder, and I hold on.

My body instantly goes to war.

A cold, blinding sheet of panic hits the back of my skull. Every single alarm in my nervous system shrieks at once. *Trap. Man. Too close. No exit. Get out.*

My lungs completely seize. The air is gone. A violent wave of nausea rolls through my stomach, and my muscles lock so tight they feel like snapping wire. My skin is freezing, prickling with pure, unadulterated terror. My brain is screaming at me to shove him backward, to scramble away, to run until I hit a wall.

I don't move.

I grip the back of his flannel shirt in both fists. I am shaking. It's not a tremble. It's a brutal, full-body shudder. My teeth are actually chattering against his collarbone. I am vibrating so hard the bed frame squeaks.

*This is Cal,* I tell my screaming nerves. *This is Cal. This is Cal.*

Beneath me, Cal has turned to stone.

He is entirely rigid. He has stopped breathing. He doesn't put his arms around me. He doesn't touch me at all. I can feel his hands hovering in the empty air beside my ribs, terrified to land, terrified to close the cage my body thinks I'm in.

"I'm here," I whisper into his neck.

My voice is ragged. It barely makes it out of my throat.

I am sweating. I am freezing. The rough fabric of his shirt is scraping my cheek, and I force myself to focus on it. Just the shirt. Just the smell of the cheap laundry pods we buy at the drugstore. Just the fact that he is not moving a single muscle to constrain me. If I wanted to run, I could.

I'm not running. I'm so tired of running in my own room.

"Nora," he whispers. His voice is completely wrecked. It sounds like it's breaking in half.

"Don't move," I gasp. I can't catch a full breath. My chest is heaving against his. "Just. Stay."

I dig my fingernails into his back. I force my weight down.

Slowly, agonizingly slowly, his hands lower. They don't grab me. They don't hold me in place. They just settle against the middle of my back. Feather-light. Just enough pressure to prove he is real.

My body violently rejects the contact. A flare of white-hot static explodes behind my eyes. I flinch hard, my shoulders jumping toward my ears, my breathing turning into sharp, panicked cuts of air.

But I don't let go of his shirt.

I squeeze my eyes shut. I press my forehead harder into his shoulder. I let myself shake, and shake, and shake, and I refuse to move an inch.

His heart is hammering against my ribs.

It's frantic. A rapid, heavy thud right beneath my own chest. He is terrified. He is sitting perfectly still, letting me use him as a grounding wire, and he is terrified I am going to shatter.

*Evidence,* my brain says. *Here is your evidence.*

My lungs are burning. I can't get enough oxygen through my tight throat. I am taking short, jagged gasps, sounding like an animal caught in a snare.

Cal takes a breath.

He draws it in slowly. Deeply. His chest expands against mine, rising steady and deliberate. He holds it for a second. Then he exhales. A long, even stream of air that ruffles my hair.

He does it again. In. Out.

He isn't speaking. He isn't telling me to calm down. He is just building a metronome. He is offering me a rhythm to copy.

I try to catch it. I try to pull a breath all the way down into my stomach. It catches in my throat and breaks into a dry sob.

His hand shifts on my back. Just a fraction of an inch. A small, circular motion with his thumb between my shoulder blades. The lightest possible touch.

My body recoils, a sharp jolt of electricity down my spine, but this time, the panic hits a wall. The adrenaline has been burning too hot, for too long. My system is starting to run dry. The shaking changes. The rigid, violent vibrating begins to loosen into a deep, exhausted trembling. My muscles feel like they've been beaten with a hammer.

"Okay," I drag the word out. It scrapes my throat. "Okay."

"I've got you," he says softly. He says it to the wall behind me. He keeps his voice low, no sudden vibrations, no sudden noises.

I loosen my death grip on his shirt. My fingers are cramped. The joints ache. I flatten my palms against his chest instead. I can feel the heat of him through the flannel. The steady rise and fall.

In. Out.

I match it. My breath shudders on the way in, but it goes deeper this time.

The alarm bells in my head are still ringing, but they are getting quieter. The static is fading. My body is exhausted, and in that exhaustion, there is finally a tiny crack of space where logic can leak back in.

*He didn't trap you.*

*He didn't hurt you.*

*You are still here.*

I drop my forehead heavily against his collarbone. All the fight drains out of me at once. I slump against him, my weight finally settling into his lap, gravity taking over.

Cal lets out a shaky exhale. His arms come around me. Not tight. Not a cage. Just a boundary. He wraps his arms lightly around my back and rests his chin on the top of my head.

I don't flinch.

I am so tired I can barely keep my eyes open, but I don't flinch. I just stay there, breathing his air, letting the radiator hiss in the corner. I let him hold me.

My face is wet.

I didn't realize that was happening. I'm not sobbing. It's not an active thing. It's just water leaving my body because my body has been squeezed too hard for too long. It soaks into the collar of his shirt, turning the red flannel dark.

Cal doesn't pull away. He doesn't try to wipe my face or ask if I need a tissue. He doesn't ask if I'm okay. He knows the answer.

Instead, he starts to rock. A millimeter forward, a millimeter back. The smallest, safest possible movement. Just enough to remind my brain that the ground is solid and we are moving together.

My muscles feel like lead. I am so heavy I feel like I'm going to fall through the floor.

"I'm sorry," I say into his neck.

I don't know what part I'm apologizing for. For making him hide in his own room for months. For ruining the coffee run. For the fact that someone else broke my locks and now Cal is the one bleeding on the glass.

Cal's arms tighten. Just a fraction.

"Don't," he says. His voice breaks entirely on the vowel. He clears his throat, but it doesn't help. "Please don't. You have nothing to be sorry for. Not one thing."

He rests his cheek on the top of my head. He is crying too. I can feel the dampness of it in my hair, the subtle hitch in his chest when he breathes. We are just two people sitting in a cramped dorm room, quietly falling apart together.

The letter is in the drawer.

Let it stay there. Let it be twelve-point font and black ink and insufficient evidence. The panel doesn't know anything. They sit in their offices and they look at timelines and they don't know what it costs just to sit on my best friend's lap. They don't know the violent, terrible math of surviving my own skin.

They don't get to tell me what happened. I know what happened. My body knows what happened. Cal knows.

I close my eyes.

The frantic buzzing in my veins is finally gone. There is only exhaustion left. A deep, hollow quiet.

I press my hand flat against the center of his chest. The rhythm is steady now. Beating against my palm. I keep my hand there, and I let him hold me, and I don't go anywhere else.
