**april, various**

I haven't written here in a while. Not because things are bad. I think because when I sit down to write I can't find the shape of it — the days have become one thing and it's hard to pull a single thread out of the fabric.

Some things:

I can do a clean in-between in about twenty minutes now. When I started it was forty-five. Mizuno-san said my spacing has improved and she doesn't say things she doesn't mean, so I believe her. I can feel the difference in my hand — the line is more confident, fewer strokes to get where I'm going. My eraser lasts longer. This is how you measure progress in douga: your eraser lasts longer.

The studio took on a contract for a show I won't name. Tight schedule — [eleven episodes in five months](https://www.nippon.com/en/in-depth/d01174/). We're doing cleanup and in-betweens for the first six. I've been at my desk from 9am to 11pm most days this month. Some days later. Last Tuesday I looked up and the office was empty except for Takagi-san and the lights from the printing company downstairs had gone off, which means it was past midnight.

[The pay is per drawing](https://www.nippon.com/en/in-depth/d01174/). I knew this when I started. I know the number. I did the math before I moved here and the math was — I thought it was something I could work around. I would be fast, I would be efficient, I would build up to key animation within a year or two and the rate would change.

The math this month: I completed 412 drawings. Some weeks are higher — the walking sequences go faster because I've internalized the arcs. Action cuts take longer. A character turning while their hair moves is fifteen minutes of erasing if the key frames are loose, and the key frames on this show are loose. [412 drawings at the rate they pay for douga](https://www.slashfilm.com/782183/the-pay-controversy-that-plagues-the-japanese-anime-industry/). I'll let you do the multiplication. I did it on the train last week and then I put my phone in my bag and watched my hands for a while.

I am not complaining. I want to be clear about that. I'm writing it down the same way I write down the light in the windows and the sound of pencils — because it's true and I want to remember what it felt like to be here, at this point, doing this math.

My hands: the callus on my ring finger has a callus. My right shoulder does something at the end of a long day — a tightness that starts at the base of my neck and moves down to my elbow. I stretch in the bathroom because I don't want to stretch at my desk where people can see. In the morning on the train my fingers are stiff and I open and close them and it takes until Nerima to feel normal. I am 22. I don't think this is supposed to happen at 22 but I don't know who to compare myself to. Everyone in the studio has the same hands.

The weather exchange with my grandmother: she texted "cherry blossoms are early this year" and I texted "here too" and I didn't mention that I saw them on the walk from the station at 7am and again on the walk back at midnight and those were the only times I was outside. She sends me the weather every morning. I know she's checking that I'm alive. The reply is the proof. Sometimes I reply late and I can feel her waiting.

I called home on Sunday. My mother asked if I was eating enough. I said yes. This is mostly true — I eat, just not the things she would consider eating. Lawson onigiri is food. It is technically food. My father got on the phone and said "the restaurant is busy, spring tourists" which is how he says he's fine. There was a pause where nobody said anything and I could hear the kitchen — water boiling, the clatter of bowls. I missed the sound so much I almost couldn't speak. I said "that's good" and he said "yes" and my mother took the phone back and said "sleep earlier" and I said "I will."

The thing I haven't told them: I love this. Not the hours, not the math. The work. The actual motion of the pencil, the moment when you flip the stack and a character breathes. Last week I did a cut where a girl catches a leaf — just reaches out and closes her hand around it — and the key animator had drawn the reach and the close and I drew the four frames between and when I flipped them the fingers curled and the leaf disappeared into her palm and for a second I forgot I was tired. That keeps happening. The exhaustion and the joy living in the same body, taking turns.

Takagi-san has been here eleven years. He's a key animator now — the best in the studio, maybe. He draws things I can't imagine drawing. Last week he did a rain sequence and each drop caught the light differently and I stood behind his desk and watched and he didn't notice or didn't mind. Eleven years. He has the same shoulder thing. He has the same hands. He still stays past midnight sometimes and when he leaves he says "otsukaresama" to the empty room.

I asked him once — carefully, because I ask everything carefully — if it gets easier. He said "the drawing gets easier. The rest stays the same." I don't know what "the rest" is yet. I think I'm starting to.

The ginkgo outside the station has new leaves. Very small, very green. I noticed them yesterday morning. I notice things like this — the small changes, the seasons turning over in specific trees on specific streets. I don't know if this is an animator's habit or if it's just mine. Either way: the leaves are coming back. The light in the studio is longer now. It reaches my desk by 3pm instead of 4.

I'm going to sleep. My neighbor's television is on. It's fine. It's company.
