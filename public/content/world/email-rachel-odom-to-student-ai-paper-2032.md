Hi Alyssa,

I've read your Essay 2 draft — the one on how social media affects self-perception. I want to talk about it with you before I grade it, and I want to do that in person rather than through comments on the page.

I'm going to be honest with you, because I think you deserve honesty more than you deserve a grade right now.

This draft doesn't sound like you. Your first essay — the one about your grandmother's garden — had a voice I could hear. You wrote a sentence in that essay: "She never explained why she planted marigolds around the tomatoes, she just did it, and I learned it the way you learn a song you've heard your whole life." That sentence had your fingerprints on it. It moved in a way that was specific to how *you* think. I remember it because I read it twice and wrote "yes" in the margin.

This essay has no fingerprints. The sentences are correct. The paragraphs transition smoothly. The thesis is stated clearly in the introduction and restated in the conclusion. It does everything a composition textbook says a paper should do, and it sounds like no one in particular wrote it.

I'm not going to accuse you of anything. I don't have access to any detection tool and I wouldn't use one if I did — [they're unreliable](https://nces.ed.gov/pubsearch/pubsinfo.asp?pubid=2024236), and using them would make me a worse teacher, not a better one. What I have is eight years of reading student writing. I know what a person sounds like when they're working through an idea they haven't figured out yet. I know what it looks like when someone is reaching for a thought they can almost articulate but not quite. That's the most exciting thing in the world to read in a student paper. This essay doesn't have it. It has answers where there should be questions.

Here's what I think happened, and you can tell me if I'm wrong: I think you ran out of time, or you got stuck, or you didn't know where to start, and you used a tool that gave you something that looked finished. And I think you're smart enough to know it wasn't your best work, but it was due, and something is better than nothing.

I understand that logic. I'm not angry. But I need you to hear this: the thing you handed in isn't an essay. It's the absence of one. It fills the space where your thinking was supposed to go, and now that space is full of something that isn't yours, and we've both lost something. You've lost the chance to struggle with an idea and come out the other side with something you actually made. I've lost the chance to help you get there.

I'd like to offer you this: come to my office hours on Thursday (Starbucks in the Martin building, 2:00–3:30, I'm the one with the RSA tote bag and the small coffee). Bring your notes — whatever you had before the draft. We'll talk about what you actually wanted to say about social media and self-perception, and you can rewrite the essay from that conversation. I'll grade the rewrite with no penalty. The deadline for the rewrite would be October 28.

If you don't want to do this, I understand. I'll grade what you submitted. But I think you have something to say about this topic, and I'd rather read that than what's on the page right now.

This stays between us. I'm not reporting anything to the department or the dean. I'm writing to you as your teacher, not as a compliance officer.

Let me know.

— Rachel
