November 14, 2032

Dear Dr. Vojtko,

You don't know me. We never met. I teach composition at Whitmore University in Ohio and introductory literature at Ridgemont Community College forty minutes south. You taught French at Duquesne for twenty-five years. We have the same job. I'm writing to you because there's no one else who would understand this letter without me having to explain it, and I am very tired of explaining.

I read about you in 2013. The *Post-Gazette* piece. I was in my second year of the PhD program at Indiana and I sat in the library stacks on the fourth floor and read it twice and then I didn't do anything for an hour. You were 83. You had no insurance. Your house had no heat. You collapsed on the lawn.

I was twenty-two. I thought: that won't happen to me. Not because I was smarter or more careful but because I was twenty-two and twenty-two-year-olds don't have the math yet. I had an advisor who said my work was exceptional. I had a committee that said my dissertation was one of the strongest in years. I had all of these people telling me I was going to do important work and none of them told me what the market looked like. I think they didn't know. I think some of them did know and didn't say it because they needed graduate students to teach their survey sections.

I'm forty-one. I'll tell you where I am.

I make [$19,600 a year](https://www.aaup.org/issues/contingent-faculty). Six courses, two institutions. My contract at Whitmore is semester-to-semester. I found out on August 19th that I was teaching in September. I'd already written the syllabi because I always write the syllabi. My hours are capped at [27.5 per week so the university doesn't have to offer health insurance](https://www.aaup.org/issues/contingent-faculty). I work 55. The 27.5 is a number someone picked so that a different number wouldn't apply to me.

I don't have health insurance. I had a marketplace plan for two years — [$340 a month with subsidy, $8,500 deductible](https://www.kff.org/health-reform/state-indicator/marketplace-average-benchmark-premiums/). I dropped it. I take ibuprofen. My right shoulder does something when I carry my bag but I can't afford the diagnostic let alone whatever comes after the diagnostic. You understand this arithmetic. You lived inside it for twenty-five years.

I have [$62,400 in student loan debt](https://www.nea.org/resource-library/degrees-not-debt). The interest has been accruing for nine years on income-driven repayment, which means I pay $261 a month and the balance goes up. Last quarter it went up $589. The forgiveness date is 2048. I'll be fifty-seven. The forgiven amount is taxable as income.

My rent just went up 22%. The property management company — which is owned by a [private equity firm](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-106701) whose investor letter says "turnover is a value-creation event" — sent me a notice generated by a [revenue management algorithm](https://www.propublica.org/article/yieldstar-rent-increase-realpage-rent). There is no person to call. There is no person.

I grade at the campus Starbucks because my office is a storage closet I share with twelve other adjuncts. I buy a small coffee and refill it once. A student wrote a Yelp review about me. She called me "the mystery grading lady." Another student — Marcus, who I taught three years ago — came back for an alumni event and saw me at the same table. "Five stars for the fact that some things don't change," he wrote. He doesn't know what it means that I'm still there.

I keep his letter in my bag. He wrote it on actual paper with an actual stamp. He said I was the first teacher who made him feel like his voice mattered. When the math gets bad I take it out and read it and I think: this is real. This is the thing I do and it matters.

This is the trap. You know it's the trap. I know you know because you were in it for twenty-five years and you never left. The institution knows we'll work for nothing because the work means something to us. [The students in my composition class each pay $5,000 for the course.](https://www.aaup.org/issues/contingent-faculty) I make [$3,500 to teach it](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/faculty-issues/tenure/2024/03/04/report-highlights-how-little-adjunct-professors-earn). Less than one of them pays. The class generates $150,000 in tuition revenue. My salary is a rounding error.

I apply for tenure-track jobs every October. Forty-seven applications last year. Each one takes three to five hours. Two phone interviews. No campus visits. The positions went to candidates from Michigan and Berkeley. [There are 1,200 applicants for every tenure-track line in my field.](https://www.historians.org/research-and-publications/perspectives-on-history/october-2022/the-academic-job-market-is-still-terrifying)

I tell my students on the first day of every semester that there is no better job than this. I mean it. I stand in front of thirty people and I help them become better writers and thinkers and I believe it matters more than almost anything else a person could do. And then I drive forty minutes to Ridgemont and do it again and then I go home and eat rotisserie chicken standing at a counter in a kitchen that isn't mine and I grade papers until midnight. The cat sits on the counter and watches me. Her name is Dorothea.

Here is the math, Dr. Vojtko. I am forty-one. I have no retirement account. No savings. A rented room. A cat. A bag full of papers and $62,400 in debt. The distance between you and me is twenty-five years of the same job.

I don't know what I'm asking you. I don't know what I'd want you to say. I think I just wanted to tell someone who wouldn't need it explained.

It is 1:14 AM. I have 23 papers left to grade before my 9 AM section. Dorothea is asleep on the chair. The house is quiet because Denise works overnights and is never home when I am.

I'm going to grade the papers. I'm going to write real comments on every one. I'm going to mean every word. In the morning I'll stand in front of that room and I'll teach well — not adequately, not passably, well — because it's the only thing in my life that works the way it's supposed to.

I don't know if that's enough. I don't think you knew either.

Rachel
