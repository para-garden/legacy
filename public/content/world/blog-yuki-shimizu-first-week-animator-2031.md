**first week**

I started on Monday. I want to write this down before it becomes normal.

The studio is on the second floor above a printing company. The stairwell smells like toner. When you open the door at the top the light changes — the office has a row of west-facing windows and in the afternoon everything turns gold. Nobody closes the blinds. I think it's an unspoken agreement.

My desk is in the back row, near the wall. It's old — the surface has a dip in the center from years of someone pressing down while drawing. I can feel the shape of whoever sat here before me in the wood. The desk to my left belongs to Takagi-san, who has been here eleven years. He has a small electric fan clipped to his monitor even though it's October. On his desk there is a figure of a character I don't recognize, a row of mechanical pencils in a ceramic cup, and a photo of a cat. I haven't asked about the cat yet.

The sound of the studio is pencils. Not typing — pencils on paper, all day, a soft continuous scratching that rises and falls depending on the deadline. Underneath that: the hum of the light boxes, the click of the animation supervisor flipping paper, someone's chair wheel rolling across the floor. I could fall asleep to this sound. I don't mean that as a complaint.

On Tuesday they gave me my first real cut. In-betweens for a walking sequence — a background character crossing a bridge. Seven frames. The key animator had drawn the first and last positions and I had to find the ones between. I spent four hours on it. I erased too much. The spacing was slightly off on frames three and four and Mizuno-san caught it in review. She didn't say it was wrong. She held up my drawing next to the keys and said "look at the arc of the shoulder" and I could see it immediately — the path was too flat, the shoulder should dip slightly as the weight transfers. She corrected it in two strokes. I watched her pencil and tried to memorize the motion.

Seven frames of a background character nobody will look at. But when I flipped the paper and saw them move — the shoulders shifting, the coat swinging, a person walking — something in my chest went tight. Someone will watch this scene. They won't see the bridge or the person crossing it. They'll be watching the main characters in the foreground. But the person will be walking behind them, and the walk will be right, and that's mine.

That's the reason I'm here. A scene in an anime I watched when I was seventeen — rain on a window, drawn by hand, every drop slightly different. Fourteen seconds that someone spent days on. I watched it until I could see the individual frames. I want to be the person who draws something like that. I know I'm years away. I know douga is where everyone starts. That's fine. The in-betweens are the work and the work is the path and I am on it.

I called my grandmother on Wednesday evening. I told her about the desk with the dip in it and she laughed — the particular laugh she does when she's pleased about something. She asked if I'd drawn anything good. I told her about the walking sequence. She said "seven frames. That's a start." She told me the ginkgo trees by the Hirose River are starting to turn.

It's Friday now. I'm writing this on the train home. The car is mostly empty this late. My hands smell like graphite and eraser dust.

I don't know how to end this. I don't have anything to say about the future or what it means. I just wanted to write down what the light looked like.
