## The Future of In-Betweens? AI Tools Promise 10x Efficiency for Anime Production

*By Tomohiro Saitō · Translated from Japanese*

The chronic production crisis facing the Japanese animation industry may have found an unlikely ally: artificial intelligence.

At AnimeJapan 2032, three competing AI-assisted in-betweening tools were demonstrated to packed halls — FrameForge (Aniplex subsidiary SolNote), MotionBridge (a MAPPA-backed joint venture), and the open-source DougaFlow, developed by a team at Tokyo University of Technology. All three promise the same thing: generate clean in-between frames from key animation, reducing the need for human douga artists by [60–80%](https://janica.jp/survey/survey2019Report.pdf).

The demos were impressive. MotionBridge, in particular, showed a side-by-side comparison: a walking sequence that took a human in-between animator approximately 3 hours to produce 12 frames was generated in 4 minutes. The output was clean, on-model, and — to this writer's eye — difficult to distinguish from human work at standard broadcast resolution.

The audience applauded. Not everyone in the industry shares the enthusiasm.

---

### The numbers that drive automation

The case for AI in-betweening is, on paper, overwhelming. [The Japan Animation Creators Association (JAniCA) 2019 survey](https://janica.jp/survey/survey2019Report.pdf) — the most recent comprehensive industry survey — found that the median annual income for douga (in-between) animators was approximately ¥1.25 million ($9,400), with some reporting incomes below ¥1 million. At a standard rate of [¥200 per drawing](https://janica.jp/survey/survey2019Report.pdf), an animator producing 20 drawings per day for 25 working days earns ¥100,000 per month — roughly $750.

Studios, meanwhile, are booking more projects than they can staff. [The Japan Animation Association reported 300+ TV anime series in production in 2023](https://aja.gr.jp/english/japan-anime-data), a number that has grown steadily. The gap between demand and available skilled labor has made in-betweening a persistent bottleneck. Outsourcing to studios in South Korea, Vietnam, and the Philippines has been standard practice for decades, but [quality control remains a challenge](https://aja.gr.jp/english/japan-anime-data) and costs have risen as those countries' domestic industries develop.

Into this gap: AI. If a tool can generate acceptable in-betweens from keys, the production math changes dramatically. A key animator's time becomes more leveraged. Schedules become more manageable. The chronic overwork that has [defined the industry for decades](https://janica.jp/survey/survey2019Report.pdf) becomes — theoretically — less necessary.

---

### 'The arc of the shoulder'

Not everyone is convinced the output is truly equivalent.

"I can show you a walking sequence generated by AI and a walking sequence drawn by a human, and at broadcast resolution, you won't see the difference," said Kenji Mizuno, an animation supervisor at a small Suginami-based studio who has reviewed AI-generated in-betweens for a subcontracted project. "But I can feel it. The timing is mathematically correct but perceptually wrong. The weight doesn't transfer the way a human draws weight. A body turning — the way a shoulder dips and the fabric follows a frame late — the AI gets it 90% right. That last 10% is the whole thing."

Mizuno said she was not opposed to AI tools in principle but worried about what happens to the pipeline. "Douga is where every animator starts. You learn how motion works by drawing it frame by frame. If you automate that, where do new key animators come from? The tool generates the frames. Who teaches the tool what the frames should feel like?"

---

### Industry response

The Japan Animation Creators Association has not taken a formal position on AI in-betweening tools but issued a statement in March noting that "any technology that affects the working conditions of animation creators should be evaluated with full transparency regarding its impact on employment, training, and artistic quality."

Major studios have adopted a range of positions. MAPPA's investment in MotionBridge suggests confidence; other studios, [including Kyoto Animation](https://www.kyotoanimation.co.jp/en/), have stated publicly that they have no plans to adopt AI tools for any stage of production.

Smaller studios — the ones most dependent on per-drawing contract labor — are watching closely. For a studio booking subcontract douga work at ¥200 per drawing, an AI tool that generates acceptable output at a fraction of the cost could mean survival. For the animators who currently do that work, it could mean the opposite.

---

### The frame nobody sees

At the AnimeJapan demonstration, FrameForge showed a sequence from a rural scene — a background character crossing a bridge, coat swinging. The AI generated seven in-between frames from two key poses. The coat moved. The weight transferred. The audience murmured approvingly.

Somewhere, a person had drawn that same kind of sequence — seven frames, a background character nobody watches, hours of careful work — and it was the first thing they were proud of.

The tool doesn't know that.
