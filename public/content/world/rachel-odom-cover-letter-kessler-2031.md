Dear Members of the Search Committee,

I am writing to apply for the tenure-track Assistant Professor of English position at Kessler College, with a specialization in American literature and composition, as advertised on the [MLA Job List](https://joblist.mla.org/). I hold a PhD in English from Indiana University, where my research focused on the rhetoric of self-effacement in nineteenth-century American women's diaries — the formal strategies by which writers who were not supposed to be writing created space for themselves on the page. I am drawn to Kessler because of your institutional commitment to undergraduate teaching as an intellectual practice, not a service obligation, and because your English department's integration of literary study with sustained composition instruction reflects what I believe the discipline should look like.

My dissertation, *The Modest Record: Self-Effacement and Authority in Nineteenth-Century American Women's Diaries*, examined how writers including Fanny Fern, Alice James, and Charlotte Forten Grimké deployed rhetorical modesty not as deference but as a compositional strategy — a way of claiming authority through the performance of its absence. The project contributes to ongoing conversations in American literary studies about whose voices register as intellectual and whose register as merely personal, a question that remains urgent in the contemporary writing classroom. I have presented this research at the American Literature Association annual conference and the Society for the Study of American Women Writers, and I am currently revising the dissertation's third chapter, on Forten Grimké's journals during Reconstruction, for submission to *American Literary History*.

At Whitmore University, where I have taught since 2023, I have developed and taught courses in American literature survey, first-year composition, advanced expository writing, and a topics course on women's life writing that I designed from the ground up. My composition pedagogy is rooted in the belief that writing is thinking made visible — that a composition course is not remediation but the place where students learn to build and test ideas in prose. In my first-year sections, students produce four major essays across the semester, each accompanied by a revision memo in which they articulate what changed between drafts and why. This reflective practice has been the most transformative element of my teaching. Students who arrive believing they are "bad writers" leave with a vocabulary for describing what they do on the page and a sense that their choices are choices.

I am also committed to the kind of close mentorship that a school like Kessler makes possible. Your student-to-faculty ratio and your emphasis on individualized advising are not just recruiting language to me — they describe the teaching relationship I already practice, in office hours that consistently run over, in the feedback I write on student drafts that my colleagues sometimes tell me is too much. I don't believe there is such a thing as too much. At Ridgemont Community College, where I also teach two sections per semester, I work with students who are often the first in their families to attend college, who are balancing coursework with full-time employment, and who have been told in various ways that serious intellectual work is not for them. My job is to make the room safe enough and rigorous enough that they discover otherwise. I would welcome the opportunity to bring that commitment to Kessler's student body.

Your department's curricular structure — the linked gateway sequence that pairs a literature course with a composition workshop in the first year — is, frankly, the model I would design if given the chance. It treats reading and writing as a single practice rather than adjacent requirements, and it gives students a cohort experience that sustains them through the major. I would be eager to contribute to this sequence, and I can also envision upper-division courses that would complement your existing offerings: a seminar on American autobiography and the politics of self-representation, a course on the rhetoric of institutional language (how contracts, policies, and official communications construct the subjects they address), and a workshop-based course pairing creative nonfiction with archival research.

My teaching evaluations are enclosed, along with a writing sample, curriculum vitae, and three letters of recommendation submitted through Interfolio. I would be grateful for the opportunity to discuss how my teaching and research might contribute to the English department at Kessler. I can be reached at the email address and phone number above and am available for a conversation at the committee's convenience.

I am aware that [the academic labor market in English continues to contract](https://andrewgoldstone.com/blog/job-market2023/) — that [roughly 68 percent of faculty nationally now hold contingent appointments](https://www.aaup.org/data-snapshot-contingent-faculty-us-higher-ed), that tenure-track lines disappear and are replaced by per-course positions paying [$2,500 to $5,000 per section](https://www.cupahr.org/resource/adjunct-faculty-in-the-higher-education-workforce/), that the number of tenure-eligible faculty has [fallen from 53 percent to 32 percent since 1987](https://www.aaup.org/academe/issues/spring-2025/data-snapshot-tenure-and-contingency-us-higher-education-fall-2023). I mention this not out of grievance but because Kessler's decision to hire for a tenure-track line in this market is itself a statement about what the institution values. It is a statement I take seriously. The work I have done — in classrooms that seat twenty-six, in classrooms that seat forty, in an office I share with twelve other instructors — has prepared me to honor it.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,

Rachel Odom, PhD
