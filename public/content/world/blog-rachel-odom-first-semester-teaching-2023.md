**The One Where a Freshman Ruins Your Lesson Plan (In the Best Way)**

Okay so I told myself I was going to write about the job market, because that's what this blog is supposed to be — dispatches from the tenure-track search, the cover letter mines, the existential dread of checking Interfolio at 2am. I'll get to that. But something happened on Tuesday that I need to write down first.

I taught my first class. My *own* class. Not a TA section where someone else wrote the syllabus and I'm basically a substitute teacher with a lanyard. My name is on the schedule. ENG 101, Section 04, TR 11:00–12:15, Hargrove 210. Dr. Odom. That's me. I put my name on the board and then immediately erased "Dr." and wrote "Rachel" because I panicked about seeming pretentious. Then I erased "Rachel" and wrote "Dr. Odom" again because my advisor's voice in my head said *you earned that title, use it*. Then I erased the whole thing and just started talking. Great first impression. Very authoritative.

The class is freshman comp. Twenty-six students, mostly first-years, a few sophomores retaking it. We're doing a personal essay unit — "write about a time you changed your mind about something." I gave them the prompt on Thursday and they had drafts due Tuesday and I was fully prepared for the usual. The five-paragraph thing. The thesis-at-the-end-of-paragraph-one thing. "In this essay I will discuss." I had my whole spiel ready — *what if we didn't do that, what if we tried to surprise ourselves.*

And most of them were exactly what I expected. Fine. Good, even — some of them can already write, they just don't know it yet. One girl wrote about switching from pre-med to communications and it was obviously a letter to her parents disguised as an essay, which, honestly, respect.

But then there was this kid. Marcus. Back row, baseball cap, hadn't said a word all week. I'd clocked him as a back-row-baseball-cap kid, which — I know. I know better. I literally wrote my dissertation on the rhetoric of self-effacement. I should be the last person making assumptions about who's going to surprise me.

His essay was about his grandfather. Or — it started like it was about his grandfather. It was about watching his grandfather build a fence. And the first page was just — the fence. What kind of wood. How his grandfather measured twice and didn't cut once, he measured four times, because "you can't put the wood back on." How the post hole digger worked. How the concrete mix smelled. I kept waiting for the turn, the *what I learned from this experience* paragraph, the moral. It didn't come.

Instead he wrote this sentence. I'm going to quote it because I haven't been able to stop thinking about it:

"He never told me he was teaching me something and I think that's why it stuck."

That's it. That was the whole essay. The fence. The grandfather's hands. The way he didn't explain anything, he just did the work in front of the kid and the kid watched and something transferred. And then at the end Marcus wrote — and this is the part that got me — "I didn't change my mind about anything. I just learned something I didn't know I was learning. I don't know if that counts."

I sat in my office (which is a closet, literally, it used to be a supply closet, there's still a label on the door that says CUSTODIAL and someone stuck a Post-it over it that says ENGLISH) and I read it three times. Because what he'd described — the transmission that happens when you're not performing the act of teaching, when you're just *doing the thing* and someone else is present — that's the whole game. That's what the best teaching is. And this eighteen-year-old in a baseball cap just described it better than most of the pedagogy scholarship I read in grad school.

I told him in class. Not the pedagogy part — I'm not going to be the professor who makes a freshman feel like a specimen. I just said, "This is one of the most honest pieces of writing I've read in a long time, and the ending is perfect. Don't let anyone tell you to add a conclusion." He nodded. That was it. But I could see him sit up a little in his chair for the rest of the period.

Here's the thing. I know what I'm supposed to be focused on right now. The market. The applications. I have a spreadsheet — fourteen schools so far and it's only September, I'll probably hit forty by November. I know the odds. I know what 1,200 applicants for one position looks like. I know that most of the people reading my cover letter will spend ninety seconds on it. I know that "ABD from Indiana" is not "PhD from Michigan." I know all of this.

But I was standing in Hargrove 210 on Tuesday morning and a kid who hadn't spoken all week showed me something true about how people learn, and I thought: *this is the room I'm supposed to be in.*

I don't know if Whitmore will keep me past this year. The contract is semester-to-semester. I don't know if I'll get a campus visit anywhere. I don't know where I'll be in five years. But right now — this week — I taught a class and a student surprised me and I walked to my car afterward and sat there for a minute with the engine off because I needed a second to just feel how good it was.

I'll do the job market post next week. I promise. Right now I want to sit with this one a little longer.

More soon.

—R.
