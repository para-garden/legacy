**october, nothing of consequence**

I know I haven't written. I don't have a reason. I open the page and then I close it. There's a gap between what I want to say and what I know how to say and it's been like that for a few months.

Okay. I'll just write it down.

In July the studio started using DougaFlow. It's the open-source one. The production manager — Hasegawa-san — spent a week doing tests with it and then he showed us the results and the room was very quiet. He put a human in-between sequence next to the AI output on the monitor and asked if we could tell the difference. I could. Not on every frame. On some. The weight in the turns. A hand that closes slightly too smoothly — the knuckles don't lead the way they should. The fabric. It's always the fabric.

I said this. Hasegawa-san said "yes, that's why we still need you."

So now I correct the output. This is my job. The tool generates the in-betweens and I sit at my desk and review them and fix the ones that are wrong. I mark up the frames on a tablet — they gave us tablets, which I should be grateful for, they're nice — and I draw the corrections and send them back. If the corrections are significant I redraw the whole frame. If they're minor I adjust the lines. Most of the time they're minor.

I am faster at this than I was at drawing from scratch. Everyone is. Hasegawa-san said our throughput is up 340%. He said it like it was good news. It is good news. For the studio. I understand that.

Two of the contract douga artists were not renewed in August. Nishida-san and the woman whose name I'm embarrassed to say I never learned — she sat two desks from mine and she hummed when she was concentrating and now her desk is empty and nobody hums. I don't know where they went. People don't announce these things. The desk is just empty and then after a few days someone puts a box of supplies on it and it becomes a surface instead of a place where a person sat.

Takagi-san is still here. He draws keys. The tool doesn't do keys. Not yet. He said something to me in the hallway last week — we were both leaving late, the printing company lights already off — and he said "learn keys, Shimizu-san. Learn them fast." He wasn't joking. He's never joking but this was a different kind of not-joking.

I am trying to learn keys. Mizuno-san has been letting me shadow her when she has time, which is not often because the schedule is tighter now, not looser — the clients know the tools exist and they've adjusted the deadlines accordingly. The tools were supposed to reduce overwork. What they reduced was the excuse for the timeline. The timeline shrank. The work didn't.

What I want to say, and I've been trying to say it for two months, is this:

I used to draw 412 drawings a month. By hand. Each one was mine. My line, my spacing, my hand. The callus on my ring finger was from that — from the pencil pressing into the same spot ten thousand times. The callus is still there but it's softer now. The tablet stylus doesn't press the same way.

The in-between sequence I was proudest of — a girl catching a leaf, four frames, the fingers curling closed — I drew that in April. If that scene came through today it would be generated in minutes and I would review it and probably it would be fine and I would approve it and move on. And nobody would know the difference. The scene would air. The audience would watch. The leaf would still fall.

I know the output is good. I know it's getting better. I reviewed a walking sequence last week and I couldn't find a single correction. The weight was right. The fabric follow-through was right. I sat there for ten minutes looking at frames that didn't need me and I felt something I don't have a word for. Not anger. Anger would mean I thought it was wrong. It's not wrong. It's just — the room has the same desks and the same afternoon light but the sound is different. No pencils. The sound of a studio used to be pencils and paper and the particular rhythm of someone flipping a stack. Now it's clicking. Mouse clicks and stylus taps. Quieter. More efficient. I miss the sound.

My grandmother texted this morning. "First typhoon warning in Sendai, stay safe." I texted back "sunny in Tokyo." She sent a photo of the restaurant, water on the windows. My father was behind the counter, blurred. The noodles are still by hand. The machine exists — there are noodle machines — but my father makes them by hand because he says the texture is different, and it is, and the customers can tell, or they believe they can tell, which is the same thing.

I don't know if the audience can tell. I've been thinking about this every day. I don't know if the audience can tell the difference between a frame I drew and a frame the tool drew and I corrected. If they can't, what am I protecting? If they can, for how long?

Mizuno-san said something after the Animage interview. She said "the tool doesn't know what a shoulder feels like." I think about that. I think about whether knowing what a shoulder feels like matters if the drawing looks the same. I think the answer is yes but I can't prove it and I'm afraid that unprovable things don't survive in this industry.

It is 11:47 PM. The printing company lights are off. Takagi-san left an hour ago, which is early for him. The studio is empty. I'm at my desk with the tablet and the light from the screen and the window. The ginkgo outside the station will be yellow soon. I noticed the first leaf turning on Monday.

I'm still here. I don't know what that means yet.
