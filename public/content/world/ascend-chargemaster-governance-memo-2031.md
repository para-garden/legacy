## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memo accompanies the FY2032 Charge Description Master (CDM) review package presented at the November Revenue Cycle Governance Committee meeting. It summarizes the strategic rationale for Ascend's chargemaster pricing methodology, documents the governance framework and review schedule, presents key gross-to-net performance metrics, and recommends specific rate adjustments for approval prior to the January 1, 2032 CDM effective date.

Ascend's chargemaster serves multiple distinct functions within our revenue cycle. Understanding these functions — and the tensions between them — is essential context for evaluating any proposed rate change. This document is intended to provide that context for leadership and for the record.

---

## SECTION 1: WHAT THE CHARGEMASTER IS — AND IS NOT

The CDM is not a price list in the consumer sense. It is a gross charge schedule — the starting point for a series of contractual and regulatory adjustments that result in actual collected revenue.

**Virtually no payer pays chargemaster rates.** This is by design and by contract:

- **Commercial insurers** negotiate contracted rates that typically represent 30–55% of gross charges, depending on payer, service line, and market position. These contracted rates are established through multi-year payer agreements and are renegotiated on cycle.
- **Medicare** pays according to the Medicare Fee Schedule (MFS) and MS-DRG system, which are set by CMS and are not subject to negotiation. Medicare rates are structurally below chargemaster.
- **Medicaid** pays per state fee schedule, currently averaging 68 cents on the dollar relative to Medicare, and substantially below chargemaster.
- **Self-pay patients** (uninsured, underinsured, or out-of-network) are the one population technically billed at or near chargemaster rates. In practice, Ascend's financial assistance programs, prompt-pay discounts, and negotiated self-pay settlements substantially reduce actual self-pay collections below chargemaster. Self-pay accounts represent approximately 6.2% of patient encounters and 31.4% of bad debt write-offs. Charity care write-offs under the Ascend Cares program generated $47.3M in community benefit documentation in FY2031, which is included in our tax-exempt status filings under IRC 501(r) compliance.

The chargemaster is best understood as a **negotiating ceiling and a regulatory compliance instrument**, not a transaction price.

## SECTION 2: STRATEGIC FUNCTIONS OF THE CHARGEMASTER

The CDM serves four distinct strategic functions. Rate-setting decisions must account for all four simultaneously.

### 2.1 Payer Contract Negotiation Leverage

Commercial contracts are structured as percentage discounts from chargemaster (e.g., "65% of billed charges" for surgical services) or as fee-schedule-based agreements that reference chargemaster as a floor. In either case, **a higher chargemaster increases the absolute dollar value of a negotiated contract, even when the discount percentage remains constant.**

When Ascend renegotiates contracts with BlueCross, Aetna, or United, our opening position is always referenced to gross charges. A chargemaster that reflects current cost structures and inflation — including labor cost escalation, supply chain pressures, and capital depreciation — gives our contracting team a defensible basis for rate increases that a static or underpriced CDM cannot provide.

**In short: the chargemaster is a negotiating instrument. Underpricing it limits our ability to extract appropriate contract value.**

### 2.2 Cost-to-Charge Ratio and Cost Reporting

CMS requires hospitals to report cost-to-charge ratios (CCRs) for Medicare cost reporting purposes. CCRs are used in various reimbursement calculations including outlier payment thresholds, disproportionate share adjustments, and critical access hospital designations.

A well-maintained CDM — one in which charges bear a rational relationship to cost across departments — improves the defensibility of our cost reports and reduces audit exposure. Departments with charges significantly misaligned from costs create anomalies that draw scrutiny.

The goal is not to have charges that match costs. The goal is to have **charges that bear a stable and explicable relationship to costs**, even if that relationship involves a consistent multiplier.

Our current system-wide cost-to-charge ratio is 0.21, meaning our costs represent approximately 21 cents of every dollar billed. [This is within the normal range for health systems of our size and payer mix.](https://www.healthaffairs.org/doi/10.1377/hlthaff.2015.0183) It is a number we actively manage.

### 2.3 Charge Capture Integrity

Accurate and complete charge capture — ensuring that every billable service, supply, and medication is captured in the patient encounter — depends on having a CDM that is comprehensive and current. Departments that have charges for items they actually use are more likely to capture them; departments working around gaps or stale codes tend to develop workarounds that reduce capture rates and create compliance risk.

CDM maintenance is therefore not purely a pricing exercise. It is also a charge integrity exercise. Items added to the CDM during this review cycle include updated supply codes for laparoscopic equipment, revised IV medication entries reflecting GPO pricing changes, and new codes for monitoring services that were previously bundled and undercaptured.

### 2.4 CMS Price Transparency Compliance

The Hospital Price Transparency Rule (effective January 2021, enforcement escalated 2023–2026) requires hospitals to publish machine-readable CDM files and shoppable service displays. Ascend has been in compliance since Q3 2023.

The published CDM is our gross charge list. The strategic implication: **our chargemaster is now public**. Rate decisions should account for the possibility of external scrutiny — from payers, from regulators, from media, and from patient advocates — while recognizing that the published list of gross charges is legally required and strategically manageable. The context that most payers do not pay these rates is not typically communicated alongside the published list, and we do not editorialize the publication.

Legal has reviewed our published transparency files and confirms compliance with current CMS requirements. We are monitoring proposed rule updates from CMS for any changes to the shoppable services display requirement.

The gross-to-net ratio of 33.5% reflects that Ascend collects approximately 33.5 cents per dollar of gross charges system-wide. This is consistent with peer benchmarking and reflects our payer mix: approximately 38% Medicare, 19% Medicaid, 37% commercial, and 6% self-pay/other.

The gap between gross charges and net revenue — approximately $3.24 billion in contractual adjustments — represents amounts that were billed but were never intended to be collected at those rates. This is a normal feature of hospital accounting, not a discrepancy.

## SECTION 4: RECOMMENDED FY2032 RATE ADJUSTMENTS

Following departmental review, peer benchmarking (using HFMA and Solucient comparative data), and analysis of our current payer contract structures, Revenue Cycle recommends the following adjustments for the FY2032 CDM effective January 1, 2032.

### 4.1 Recommended Overall Adjustment

**Recommended system-wide CDM rate increase: 7.8%**

This reflects:
- CPI-Medical index: +5.2% (BLS, trailing 12 months)
- Labor cost escalation (nursing, RT, imaging tech): +6.4% YoY
- Supply chain / GPO pricing increases: +8.1% YoY
- Capital depreciation on imaging equipment (3T MRI, CT fleet): amortized into applicable department charges
- Estimated impact on commercial contract negotiations: +$24–31M incremental net revenue over 24-month contract cycle, based on modeling by our managed care contracting team

A 7.8% gross charge increase at our current gross-to-net ratio of 33.5% is expected to yield approximately $40–45M in additional gross charges annually, translating to $13–16M in net revenue improvement assuming stable payer mix and contract structures.

### 4.2 Selected Line-Item Changes

The following specific adjustments are highlighted for committee awareness:

| CDM Category | Current Rate | Proposed Rate | Change | Rationale |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| CT Abdomen/Pelvis w/ Contrast | $5,820 | $6,240 | +7.2% | Equipment amortization, contrast agent cost |
| OR Time — Base Hour | $3,900 | $4,200 | +7.7% | Nursing labor, sterile supply inflation |
| Medical/Surgical Room & Board | $2,600 | $2,800 | +7.7% | Staffing ratio requirements, facility costs |
| IV Normal Saline 1000ml | $352 | $380 | +7.9% | Supply chain / shortage premium |
| Aspirin 325mg (unit dose) | $44 | $47 | +6.8% | Unit dose dispensing, pharmacy overhead allocation |
| Sterile Surgical Gloves (pair) | $87 | $93 | +6.9% | PPE supply chain, sterile processing costs |
| ED Level 5 Visit | $3,580 | $3,840 | +7.3% | Acuity-adjusted labor model |

It should be noted that medications such as unit-dose aspirin and acetaminophen are charged not at acquisition cost — which may be cents per unit — but at a rate that reflects pharmacy overhead allocation: dispensing labor, pharmacist review, unit-dose repackaging, medication administration documentation, and formulary management costs. This is standard industry practice and is consistent with our cost accounting methodology.

### 4.3 Items Held Flat

The following categories are recommended to remain flat for FY2032, pending contract renegotiation outcomes:

- Evaluation and management (E&M) codes (ED and inpatient), where we are mid-cycle on BlueCross and United contracts and recommend preserving negotiating flexibility
- Pathology processing, where peer benchmarking suggests our rates are already at the 85th percentile
- Observation services, where CMS has signaled potential rulemaking that may affect how these charges are applied

## SECTION 6: REGULATORY AND COMPLIANCE NOTES

**IRC 501(r):** Our charity care write-offs under the Ascend Cares Financial Assistance Program are documented and submitted annually as required by IRS Form 990, Schedule H. FY2031 charity care of $47.3M is [calculated at gross charge rates, as permitted](https://www.gao.gov/assets/gao-06-798.pdf), which results in a larger reported community benefit figure than would result from cost-based calculations. This is standard practice.

**No Surprises Act:** The NSA continues to generate administrative complexity, particularly around anesthesia (Pinnacle Anesthesia Partners) and out-of-network disputes. NSA arbitration outcomes have been tracked by our managed care team; to date, arbitration has generally favored Ascend positions. We will continue to monitor legislative developments.

**CMS Price Transparency:** Our machine-readable file has been updated to reflect current CDM rates. Following the July 2030 CMS enforcement action against three peer systems for deficient price transparency files, we completed an external audit of our published data in August 2031. No deficiencies were identified.

**Medicaid Supplemental Payments:** Ohio Medicaid upper payment limit (UPL) calculations continue to reference our gross charge rates. Maintaining appropriately elevated charges supports our UPL position and associated supplemental payment eligibility.
