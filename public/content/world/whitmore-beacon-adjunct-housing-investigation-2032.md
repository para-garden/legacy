## "The Math Doesn't Work": Adjuncts and the Cost of Teaching at Whitmore

*By Alyssa Torres · Staff Reporter*

---

The woman at the Starbucks in Martin Academic Building is always there.

I know her. Or I thought I did. She's the one with the canvas bag who sits by the window and grades papers for hours. I wrote a [Yelp review](https://www.yelp.com) about her once — called her "the mystery grading lady." She looked up from her papers one day when I was stressed and said "whatever it is, it'll be okay" and went back to work. I didn't think about it again.

Her name is Dr. Rachel Odom. She has a PhD from Indiana University. She teaches four sections of composition at Whitmore and two sections of introductory literature at Ridgemont Community College, forty minutes south.

She grades at Starbucks because her office — Room 412B in Hargrove Hall — is a repurposed storage closet she shares with twelve other adjunct instructors.

She makes [$3,500 per course](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/faculty-issues/tenure/2024/03/04/report-highlights-how-little-adjunct-professors-earn).

---

### The numbers

I started looking into this for a class assignment — Dr. Marcus Chen's Journalism 201 — and what I found is the kind of thing you can't stop looking into once you start.

At Whitmore, [approximately 68% of all credit-bearing courses](https://www.aaup.org/issues/contingent-faculty) are taught by adjunct, visiting, or contingent faculty. This is not unusual. Nationally, the American Association of University Professors reports that [over 75% of all instructional positions in higher education](https://www.aaup.org/issues/contingent-faculty) are now off the tenure track.

The Whitmore University [2032 Viewbook](https://nces.ed.gov/collegenavigator/) — the brochure sent to prospective students and their families — describes "world-class instruction from dedicated faculty." It features a testimonial from an alumnus named Marcus T. who credits "my composition professor" with changing the way he thinks about writing.

That composition professor is an adjunct paid $3,500 per course.

Full-time tuition at Whitmore is [$54,200 per year](https://nces.ed.gov/collegenavigator/). A student taking four courses per semester pays approximately $6,775 per class. In Dr. Odom's section of ENG 102, with 30 students, the university collects roughly [$203,250 in tuition revenue](https://www.aaup.org/issues/contingent-faculty). Dr. Odom receives $3,500 — approximately 1.7% of the tuition her students pay.

I asked the Whitmore Office of Communications for comment on adjunct compensation. A spokesperson said: "Whitmore University values the contributions of all its faculty. Adjunct compensation is reviewed regularly and reflects market conditions across higher education." The spokesperson declined to provide specific figures.

---

### What $3,500 means

Dr. Odom asked that I not write about her by name. I'm using it because the numbers are the same whether or not I use her name, and I think students deserve to know who's teaching them and what they're paid.

At $3,500 per course, teaching four sections at Whitmore and two at Ridgemont, Dr. Odom earns $19,600 per year before taxes. She [does not have health insurance](https://www.aaup.org/issues/contingent-faculty) — Whitmore caps adjunct hours at 27.5 per week, below the threshold for benefits eligibility. She has [$62,400 in student loan debt](https://www.nea.org/resource-library/degrees-not-debt) from the PhD the university requires her to hold.

Her rent increased [22% this year](https://www.propublica.org/article/yieldstar-rent-increase-realpage-rent). Her apartment building was acquired by Greystone Property Management, a subsidiary of Heron Capital Partners, a [private equity firm](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-106701) whose investor letter describes tenant turnover as "a value-creation event."

I asked her why she stays.

"Because I'm good at this," she said. "Because what happens in that classroom matters. Because Marcus wrote me a letter three years ago and said I was the first teacher who made him feel like his voice mattered, and I keep that letter in my bag, and when the math gets bad I read it."

She paused.

"I know what that sounds like. I know it sounds like I'm letting them exploit me because I love the work. The thing is — both of those things are true at the same time. The work is real. The exploitation is also real. I don't know how to fix that."

---

### What the university says vs. what the university does

The [Whitmore President's Annual Report for 2032](https://nces.ed.gov/collegenavigator/) states that the university's endowment is $412 million, up 8.3% year-over-year. The report describes investments in "the student experience" including a $14.2 million renovation of the athletics complex and a new student wellness center.

The report does not mention adjunct faculty compensation.

I contacted three full-time, tenure-track professors in the English department. All three declined to comment on the record. One said, off the record: "Everyone knows. We all know what the adjuncts are paid. We know they teach most of the classes. We know the math. There's a reason nobody talks about it."

Another said: "If every adjunct at Whitmore stopped teaching tomorrow, the university would shut down. That's not an exaggeration. We don't have enough tenure-track faculty to cover the course load. But somehow that leverage never translates into better conditions."

---

### Who this is about

I want to be honest about why I'm writing this.

I took ENG 101 last year. My professor was an adjunct — not Dr. Odom, someone else. She was excellent. She read every paper carefully. She wrote comments that made me want to revise instead of just checking the grade. She held extra office hours during finals week. I assumed she was well-compensated for this. I assumed she had an office, health insurance, job security. I assumed the university that charges me $54,200 a year was paying the people who teach me.

I was paying [$6,775 for that class](https://nces.ed.gov/collegenavigator/). My professor was paid $3,500. I was paying almost twice what she earned.

I didn't know. Now I do.
