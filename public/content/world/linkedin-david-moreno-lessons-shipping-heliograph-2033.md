I've been quiet on here for a while. I owe you all an update — and some reflections.

We shipped Heliograph in January. If you've played it, thank you. If you haven't, I'm biased — but it's something special.

Shipping a game is never easy, but this one tested me in ways I didn't expect. I want to share some of what I learned, not because I have all the answers, but because I think our industry needs more honesty about what leadership actually looks like in the hard moments.

**1. Transparency isn't a one-time decision. It's a discipline.**

During our final milestone push, I made a commitment to be honest with my team about what the timeline required. No sugarcoating. People respect you more when you tell them the truth, even when the truth is uncomfortable. I wrote an email to all-studio that laid out exactly what we were asking and why. That email is the thing I'm most proud of from the entire project.

**2. People will surprise you if you let them.**

I've been in this industry eighteen years. Heliograph's team was the most talented group I've ever worked with. When we needed them to step up, they stepped up. Not because we forced them — because they cared about the work. You can't manufacture that. You can only create the conditions for it.

**3. The hardest decisions are the right ones.**

I won't pretend the last few months have been easy. Our team went through a significant restructuring in April. I can't say much about the specifics, but I want to say this: every person who was part of Heliograph's journey — whether they're still at Arclight or not — contributed something real. Their work is in the game. That doesn't go away.

If you're a hiring manager reading this: some of the most talented environment artists, animators, programmers, and designers in the industry are on the market right now. Reach out. You won't regret it.

**4. Take care of your people. And take care of yourself.**

I learned this one the hard way. I wasn't sleeping. I wasn't eating well. I was telling my team to take breaks while skipping my own. That's not leadership — that's performance. I'm doing better now. I'm talking to someone. I'm encouraging every leader in our industry to do the same.

**5. Remember why you're here.**

There's a moment — maybe two weeks after ship — when you're sitting in a quiet studio and you pull up a stream and someone is playing your game. And they reach a part of the world your team built, and they stop and look around, and they say "oh wow." That's it. That's the whole thing.

I'm proud of Heliograph. I'm proud of everyone who touched it. And I'm excited about what's next.

If you're in the Austin game dev community and want to grab a coffee — DMs are open. Always happy to talk shop, share what I've learned, or just listen.
