### Portfolio Strategy

Fund III continues to execute on its core thesis: **acquire undervalued workforce housing in markets with limited new supply, implement operational improvements via Greystone, and drive rent growth through value-add capital expenditure and [revenue management optimization](https://www.nmhc.org/).**

Key initiatives this quarter:

**Rent optimization.** Greystone's proprietary Revenue Management System (RMS) has been deployed across 100% of Fund III properties. The system analyzes [comparable market rents, vacancy rates, lease expiration clustering, and resident payment history](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/americas-rental-housing) to set unit-level pricing at renewal. Since deployment in Q3 2030, RMS-driven renewals have averaged 14.2% rent growth, compared to 6.8% for manually priced renewals. We believe significant headroom remains — our portfolio rents average [23% below estimated market-rate new construction rents](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/americas-rental-housing) in the same submarkets.

**Capital improvements.** $12.8M in value-add capex was deployed across the portfolio in Q2, focused on unit turns (updated flooring, appliances, fixtures) and common area upgrades. Renovated units command a [rent premium of $85–$140/month](https://www.nmhc.org/) over unrenovated units, representing a 12–18 month payback on per-unit renovation costs of $8,500–$12,000.

**Expense management.** Operating expenses were flat year-over-year on a per-unit basis despite a [7.2% increase in property insurance premiums](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/americas-rental-housing) and a 4.8% increase in property taxes. Greystone has achieved this through headcount optimization (reducing on-site maintenance staff from 1 per 80 units to 1 per 110 units) and centralized procurement.

### Occupancy and Turnover

Occupancy declined 1.6 percentage points year-over-year, driven primarily by increased turnover at properties where rent adjustments exceeded 15%. This is within our modeled range and consistent with the Fund's [optimization strategy](https://www.nmhc.org/): we target the rent level at which the marginal revenue from higher rents on renewing residents exceeds the cost of vacancy and turnover.

In plain terms: a resident who vacates after a 20% increase creates a unit that can be renovated and re-leased at a further premium. [Turnover is not always a negative signal — it can be a value-creation event.](https://www.nmhc.org/)

We monitor [affordability thresholds](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/americas-rental-housing) carefully. Our target resident is a household earning [$35,000–$60,000](https://www.nmhc.org/research-insight/research-notes/2023/defining-workforce-housing/), for whom our properties represent the most accessible quality housing in their submarket. These residents have limited alternatives, which supports retention even at higher rent levels.

### Market Outlook

The macro environment remains favorable for our strategy. [New multifamily construction starts have declined 28% from their 2022 peak](https://www.census.gov/construction/nrc/), tightening supply in our target markets. Wage growth in our resident demographic has been [3.1% year-over-year](https://www.bls.gov/oes/current/oes_nat.htm), providing some capacity for rent absorption — though we note that rent growth has significantly outpaced wage growth across the portfolio.

We continue to see attractive acquisition opportunities as smaller operators face [rising interest rates and maturing debt](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/americas-rental-housing). Fund III has approximately $120M of remaining investment capacity and is actively underwriting three properties totaling 1,800 units.
