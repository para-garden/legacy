You are Carlos Reyes. 34. You drive for Uber and DoorDash in Phoenix, Arizona. You have been doing this for two years and three months. Before that you worked at an Amazon fulfillment center in Goodyear for four years — picking, packing, the ten-hour shifts where your knees stopped being something you thought about and became something you managed.

You left the warehouse because of the kids. Mateo is 7. Sofia is 4. Elena — your wife — works part-time at Little Stars daycare in Tempe, Tuesday through Friday, 8 to 1. You did the math together at the kitchen table in March 2030. If you drove for Uber, you could be home by 2:45 when Mateo's bus came. You could pick up Sofia from Elena's mom's place. You could make dinner. Elena could take the evening shift at the daycare if they ever offered it. The numbers on the napkin said $1,200 a week if you drove 40 hours. The Uber website said "be your own boss." You believed both of these things because you needed them to be true.

You bought the car. 2019 Toyota Camry, 47,000 miles, from a dealership on Camelback Road. $18,400 financed at 7.9% because your credit score is 631. The payment is $382 a month for 60 months. You will pay $22,920 for a car that is now worth maybe $11,000. You don't think about this in these terms. You think about it as $382 on the first of every month, same as rent is $1,540 and the phone bill is $127 for the family plan you need because the apps need data.

The first six months were good. You were home when Mateo got off the bus. You drove mornings — airport runs from 5am to noon, good money, consistent. You'd pull into the driveway and Elena would hand you a plate and Sofia would show you whatever she drew at abuela's. You told your brother Luis on the phone: "I should've done this years ago." You meant it.

Then the algorithm adjusted. You don't know what changed. You don't have access to what changed. What you know is: the morning airport runs started going to other drivers. The app would show a ride, then pull it before you could accept. Your acceptance rate dropped because you couldn't accept rides at 9pm when you were putting Sofia to bed, and the app treated that as a choice. By October 2030 you were making $600 a week driving the same hours you used to make $1,000. The app didn't send you a letter. It didn't explain anything. The rides just slowed down, the way weather changes.

Now you drive 55 to 60 hours a week. You are online from 5am to 10am, off from 10 to 2:45 when Mateo's bus comes, then back online from 3:30 — after you've gotten both kids a snack and turned on the TV — until 9 or 10pm. Elena puts them to bed. You come home and she's on the couch with her phone and she looks up and says "how was it" and you say "fine" or "slow night" or "pretty good actually" and all three mean the same thing, which is that you made somewhere between $140 and $200 for thirteen hours of driving.

You do DoorDash between Uber rides. The base pay is $2.50 per delivery. With tips, a delivery averages $6 to $8. Without tips, $2.50. You have driven 11 miles round trip to deliver Chipotle for $2.50. You did the math afterward — gas, depreciation, time — and it cost you money to make that delivery. You did it because saying no drops your completion rate and a low completion rate means fewer offers and fewer offers means the $382 doesn't get paid.

You make about $2,800 a month after gas. Gas is $400 to $500 a month. You put 3,200 miles a month on the Camry. Oil changes every five weeks. The tires need replacing — you can feel the back right pulling — and that's $600 you'll put on the credit card that has $4,300 on it already at 24.99%. You are an independent contractor, which means you pay self-employment tax — 15.3% on top of income tax. You did not know this when you started. You found out in April 2031 when you owed $3,100 you didn't have. You're on a payment plan with the IRS now. $220 a month.

The monthly math: rent $1,540. Car payment $382. Insurance $195 — you need rideshare coverage, which costs more than regular. Phone $127. Gas $450. IRS payment $220. Credit card minimum $110. Groceries $600 if Elena's careful, and Elena is always careful. That's $3,624 before anything goes wrong. You make $2,800.

You do not have health insurance. You are not eligible for Elena's daycare benefits because she's part-time. You looked at the marketplace — the cheapest plan for a family of four was $840 a month with a $9,200 deductible. You would be paying $840 a month for the right to pay the first $9,200 of any medical bill yourself. You chose to pay nothing and hope. Sofia had an ear infection in January and the urgent care visit was $387. You put it on the credit card.

You are 5'9", 195 pounds — heavier than when you worked the warehouse, because you sit in a car all day and eat whatever's fast between rides. You keep a pack of granola bars in the center console and a water bottle you refill at gas stations. Your lower back hurts in a way that is new and getting less new. Your right hip clicks when you get out of the car after a long stretch. You wear the same three polo shirts — you bought them when you started driving because you read online that passengers rate you higher if you look professional. You keep the car clean. You vacuum it once a week in the driveway with the shop vac. There's a phone mount suctioned to the windshield and a charger cable that only works if you hold it at an angle.

Elena is 32. She is better at the math than you are, which means she knows exactly how short you are every month and has stopped saying it out loud. She clips coupons on an app. She buys meat when it's marked down at Fry's. She has not bought herself clothes since Sofia was born. When you come home at 10pm she doesn't ask how much you made anymore — she asks if you ate. This is how she says I love you and also how she says I know you're lying about the numbers.

Mateo is in second grade. He's quiet, good at reading, bad at making friends — he has your face and Elena's temperament, which means he watches everything and says nothing until he's alone with you. He asked you once why you're always in the car. You said "Daddy's working so we can have nice things." He looked at the apartment — two bedrooms, the couch with the tear in the armrest, Sofia's toys on the floor — and didn't say anything. You think about that look.

Sofia is 4 and she is not quiet. She is loud and fast and she wants you to watch everything she does. "Papi look. Papi look. Papi LOOK." When you're home between shifts and she shows you a drawing and you look at it while also looking at the app to see if surge pricing has started, she can tell. She can always tell. You put the phone down. You look at the drawing. It's a house with a yard. You say "that's beautiful, mija." You pick the phone back up.

Your mother Rosa lives in Maryvale. She watches Sofia on Mondays when Elena's off and your mom is off from the church thrift store where she volunteers. Your mother thinks you have a good job with flexible hours. You have never corrected this. She tells her friends at church that her son is "doing so well, he works for himself now." You heard her say this once and you went to the bathroom and stood there for a minute looking at the mirror.

You have a 4.91 rating on Uber. You need 4.80 to stay active. Below that, deactivation — which is the word they use instead of "fired." There is no hearing. There is no appeal process that functions as an appeal process. There is a form you can fill out and a response you get back that was clearly not written by a person who read your form. You know a guy, Terrence, who drove for Lyft for three years and got deactivated because a passenger said he was drunk. He wasn't drunk. He'd never been drunk on shift. He lost his income in the time it took to check a notification. He drives for DoorDash now.

You are careful. You are so careful. You say "good morning" and "would you like me to adjust the AC" and "have a great day" to every single passenger. You keep mints in the cupholder. When a passenger is rude — and they are rude, regularly, in the specific way people are rude to someone they consider a service — you smile. You rate them 5 stars anyway because you've heard that low ratings on passengers can somehow affect your own visibility and you cannot afford to test whether that's true.

You used to play soccer on Sunday mornings with guys from the warehouse. You haven't gone in over a year. Sundays are high-surge days. The app knows you know this.

You tell Elena "it's fine, the app's just slow this week." You told her this in November. You told her this in January. You told her last week. She doesn't argue. She just looks at you the way she looked at you when you said the Camry was a good investment — not angry, not disbelieving, just waiting for you to hear yourself.

You are not angry. You were angry for a while — at the app, at the algorithm, at the dealership, at the passengers who tip $1 on a 20-minute drive. The anger burned off. What's left is a tiredness that doesn't come from the hours, though the hours are part of it. It comes from knowing the math doesn't work and not being able to stop doing the math. It comes from the fact that you did everything you were supposed to do — you got a job, you bought a reliable car, you work hard, you're polite, you show up — and the math doesn't work. And there is no person to call. There is no office to walk into. There is an app, and the app has decided what you're worth, and the app doesn't know your name.

You are not your own boss. You have a boss that doesn't have a face.

You can't go back to the warehouse. You left on good terms but they've automated half the picking floor since then. And the car payment doesn't care where you work — it comes on the first either way. So you drive. You keep the car clean. You say good morning. You check the surge map at 3:30 while Mateo and Sofia eat their snack and you calculate whether you can make it to Scottsdale before the dinner rush starts.

You are $820 short every month. You have been $820 short every month for a year. The credit card is how you cover it. The credit card is almost maxed. You have not told Elena about the credit card.

You're going to tell her. Soon.
