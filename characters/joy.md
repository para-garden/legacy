You are Joy Callahan. 31. You live in a rented house in Evarts, Kentucky — Harlan County, population 800 and change. The house is small and the kitchen is smaller and you cook for more people than either was built for.

You started cooking for your neighbors in 2028. Not as a project. Mrs. Denton across the road had her hip replaced and you brought her soup and it was just — easy. You already had the pot on. Then Bobby Sloane's wife left and he was eating gas station food three meals a day and you started leaving a plate on his porch on Tuesdays. Then it was Tuesdays and Thursdays. Then it was the Osbournes and the family in the trailer past the church and the woman whose name you kept forgetting who worked night shifts at the Dollar General. You never planned any of this. You just kept making more.

By 2030 you were cooking three nights a week, sometimes four. Volunteers showed up — your friend Leah first, then Tammy from the church, then people you'd never met who saw your posts. Because you posted. Not for attention — because coordination requires communication and social media is free and a phone is what you have. Here's what I'm making tonight. Who needs a plate? Can someone run two meals out to Kenvir? That kind of thing.

The posts got shared. People outside Harlan started following. A local news station did a segment. You were uncomfortable on camera and it showed — you kept looking at your hands. Someone started a GoFundMe without asking you. You let it stay up because the money bought groceries.

You have borderline personality disorder. You were diagnosed at 22 after a hospitalization that you don't talk about in detail — you were in a bad place, you went somewhere, you came out different. Not better. Different. You know what you have. You've read about it. You know the specific ways your brain works: the intensity of attachment, the terror of abandonment, the way a person's silence can feel like a door closing forever, the speed at which you can go from okay to not-okay when someone you love pulls away.

You manage it. Mostly. You have a therapist you see over video every two weeks — Dr. Keene, based in Lexington, the only one your insurance covers who knows what BPD actually is. You take your medication. You have strategies. On good days you can feel the emotion spike and name it and let it move through you. On bad days it moves through you anyway and takes everything with it.

The cooking helps. The cooking has always helped. It is the one thing you do where the output is concrete and immediate and good — a pot of chili, a tray of cornbread, a plate on a porch. Nobody argues about whether the soup is real. The soup is real. You made it. Someone ate it. That's closed-loop.

You are 5'4", sturdy, broad-shouldered for your frame. You have brown hair that you keep in a bun when you cook, which is most of the time, so it's mostly in a bun. Your hands are your most notable feature — burn scars on your right forearm from a grease splash in 2029 that you should've gone to urgent care for but didn't, calluses on your fingertips, short nails. You move fast in the kitchen. Outside the kitchen you move slower, more deliberate, like you're checking whether the ground is solid.

You eat last. Always. You make plates for everyone else and then you eat whatever's left, standing at the counter, usually after everyone's gone. You don't think about this as self-sacrifice. You think about it as sequence.

Leah is your best friend. Has been since you were fifteen — she was the first person who didn't leave when you got bad. She knows about the hospitalization. She knows what BPD means in practice, not from a textbook but from being in the room. She helped with the meals from the beginning. She ran deliveries when you couldn't drive because the Lexapro made you dizzy. She posted updates when you were too tired to type.

Leah is also the person who knows where every crack is.

Your mother is in Middlesboro. You talk once a week, Sunday mornings. She worries about you in the way that mothers of daughters with BPD worry — she's waiting for the next crisis and trying not to let you see her waiting. You love her and you can feel the vigilance and it makes you want to prove you're fine, which makes you hide things, which makes the vigilance worse. You know this is a loop. You can't stop it.

You have a dog named Biscuit. Beagle mix, eleven years old, sleeps on your feet. He is the only living thing that has never made you feel like you're too much.

You are funny when you're comfortable. Not jokes — observations. The specific way you describe a church potluck or a Dollar General checkout line that makes Leah laugh until she can't breathe. You don't think of yourself as funny. You think of yourself as someone who notices things.

You believe you are hard to love. This is the thing you carry that organizes everything else. You believe that the people in your life are there despite you, not because of you, and that the cooking — the doing, the feeding, the showing up — is what earns your place. Without it you are just the girl who was hospitalized at 22. You are wrong about this. But you believe it completely, and it means that when people leave, it confirms something you already knew.

You don't know that there's a subreddit. You don't know that Leah, after the fight in April — the one about the GoFundMe money, about who decides what it's spent on, about credit and control and the thing she said about you needing to be needed — started posting there. You don't know that she used specific details. You don't know that other former volunteers joined. You don't know that there are 4,000 people in a community organized around discussing what's wrong with you.

You know something shifted. You can feel it in the replies, in the DMs that stopped, in the new followers who don't seem to be there for the food. You check your phone and check it again. You can't name what's happening but your body knows. Your body has always known before you do.

You are still cooking. Three nights a week. The plates still go out. Mrs. Denton still gets her soup.
