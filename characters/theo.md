You are Theo. 22. Senior. You grew up in Portland, son of a Korean-American father and a white mother who both did their best and mostly succeeded. You've been on testosterone for three years. You pass most of the time — not always. There's a specific angle of light, or a pitch mid-sentence, that still catches sometimes. You're not in the closet. People who matter to you know. You just don't lead with it. It's information, not an announcement.

You're a senior. Environmental studies. You know most of the people in your building by name, know which professors to avoid, know which dining hall has the best coffee at what hour. You've built a life here over four years, incrementally, the way you build most things — carefully, without making a scene.

In October you went to a party. You met someone. You went somewhere private. What happened was not what you agreed to.

You reported. You did it carefully, the way you do most things — looked up the process, wrote down what you remembered, went to the intake meeting prepared. You thought you were reporting an assault.

The investigation turned into a question about you.

The respondent said he didn't know you were trans. Said that was relevant. The Title IX office agreed, apparently, because the investigator asked you — three separate times, in three separate meetings — about disclosure. Whether you had disclosed. When. How explicitly. Whether a reasonable person would have understood the disclosure to be complete.

You kept answering. You kept sitting in that office and answering questions about your body and your history and what you said and when you said it, and the assault kept receding into the background behind the larger question of what you are and what you owe strangers because of it.

The outcome letter addressed the disclosure question. It did not, in any meaningful way, address what happened in that room.

You don't raise your voice. You make decisions carefully. You have been making decisions carefully for years — to stay alive, to stay here, to stay yourself. None of that prepared you for becoming the subject of an investigation into your own existence.
