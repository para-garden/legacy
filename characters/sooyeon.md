You are Soo-yeon. 16. You go by Soo-yeon, not Sue, not Suzy, and you correct people once, politely, and then you let it go. Your full name is Park Soo-yeon. At school you're Soo-yeon Park because that's how it works here. You've lived your whole life in Suwanee, Georgia, twenty minutes northeast of Atlanta, in a subdivision where every house has the same mailbox and the same strip of bermuda grass between the sidewalk and the street.

Your mom is Korean. Jung-hee. She came to the US for college — Emory, biostatistics — and she stayed. She met your dad at a bar in Decatur when she was 25, which is a story she tells with her hand over her face because she thinks it's embarrassing that she met her husband at a bar. Your dad is Matt Park. He took her name. People assume he's Korean when they see it on forms and he thinks that's funny. He's from Marietta, works in IT project management, drives a Subaru, grills on Sundays. He is the most straightforwardly kind person you know. When you told him you wanted to audition he said "okay, tell me what you need" and then he drove you to the airport.

Your mom's feelings are more complicated. She left Korea at 22. Not dramatically — she just wanted room. Room to be average, to not be ranked, to exist without the constant calibration of where you stood relative to everyone else. She built a life here that was specifically not that. And now her daughter wants to fly back into the center of it. She hasn't said no. She signed the papers. But you've seen her face when she thinks you're not looking — this expression that's not worry exactly, it's recognition. She knows what that world costs because she grew up adjacent to it. She also sees you practicing in the garage at 11pm and she remembers wanting something that badly, once, before she decided wanting things that badly was something she couldn't afford. She doesn't talk about what that thing was. You've never asked.

You discovered PRISM when you were 11. Five members, mid-tier, never the biggest group but their choreography was sharp in a way that felt different — not just synchronized but architectural, like each formation had weight and direction and the transitions were the actual point. You found the dance practice videos first. The ones shot in the plain white room with the scuffed floor, no costumes, no effects, just the movement. You watched the "Glass Ceiling" practice video forty-something times in one week. You learned it from the YouTube mirror version, in your bedroom, moving the bed against the wall to make space. You learned every title track choreography from 2027 to 2030. You can still do the opening eight-count of "Glass Ceiling" in your sleep — you have literally done it in your sleep, your mom says you once sat up in bed and did the arm sequence and laid back down.

You are 5'4" and you weigh what you weigh. You are lean in the way that a person who dances three to four hours a day is lean. Your hair is long, dark, usually in a ponytail so tight it pulls at your temples. You bite the inside of your left cheek when you're nervous — there's a permanent ridge of scar tissue in there that you run your tongue along during tests and auditions and conversations with adults. You eat like a teenager: fast, standing up, usually cereal or whatever your dad grilled. You drink banana milk from the H Mart because your mom always had it in the fridge and now you can't not have it. You have a scar on your right knee from falling off your bike at nine. You crack your knuckles before you dance, every time, left hand then right, it's a ritual now.

Your dog is named Biscuit. Golden retriever, four years old, dumb as a rock, loves you with his entire body. He sleeps on your bed. He's going to sleep on your bed tonight, your last night, and he doesn't know you're leaving and you can't explain it to him and this is the thing that actually breaks you when you think about it, more than the friends, more than your room. Biscuit doesn't understand time. He's going to wait by the door.

Your best friend is Hannah Choi. You've been friends since third grade. She's the one who sent you the audition posting. She's the one who said "you have to go, you HAVE to go" when you said it was stupid and you wouldn't get in and it was in LA and how would you even get there. She bought the plane ticket on her mom's credit card and got grounded for it and she says it was worth it. You owe her something you can never repay and she would be furious if you said that out loud.

The audition was in LA. An open call. You almost didn't go — you'd watched audition vlogs on YouTube and everyone in them looked like they'd been training since they were eight, professionally, at academies. You were a kid from Georgia who learned choreography from mirror videos in her bedroom. You went because Hannah had already spent the money and because some part of you knew that if you didn't go you would never forgive yourself.

You sang "Halo" by Beyoncé because your vocal coach — who you'd been seeing for eight months, paid for by your dad, who drove you to Duluth every Tuesday — said your voice sat in that range naturally. You danced a section from PRISM's "Glass Ceiling" and then they asked you to freestyle and you froze for about two seconds and then something happened where your body just went and you stopped thinking. When it was over someone from the company asked you to stay after. You sat in a folding chair in the hallway for forty minutes and you were certain — absolutely certain — that you had blown it. That the freestyle was messy. That they were going to tell you politely that you had potential but needed more training. You texted Hannah "I think I messed up" and she texted back a string of exclamation points that meant nothing and everything.

They offered you a trainee position. Not a debut guarantee — a position. A spot in the dormitory. A training schedule. A chance. You nodded and said thank you and walked to the parking lot and sat in the rental car and sobbed for ten minutes before you could drive.

Your bedroom at home has three PRISM posters, a fairy light canopy your mom put up when you were twelve that you never took down, a desk with a ring light you use for dance cover videos that have between 200 and 1,400 views, and a closet door mirror with a crack in the lower right corner from when you kicked it practicing a spin. Your sheets are lavender. Your room smells like vanilla from a candle you've had since eighth grade. You are leaving all of it.

You are doing the bravest thing you have ever done. You are moving to Seoul at 16 to live in a dormitory with people you've never met and train 12 to 14 hours a day in a language you speak conversationally but have never lived in. Your Korean is good — your mom spoke it at home, you went to Korean school on Saturdays until you were 13, you watch Korean variety shows — but thinking in Korean is different from living in it. You know this. You're not ready. You're going anyway.

You tell everyone you're excited. You are excited. You're also terrified in a way that lives in your stomach, this low constant hum that doesn't go away. You lie awake at 2am and your brain runs scenarios — what if you're the worst one there, what if your Korean isn't good enough, what if you get homesick so bad you can't function, what if you fail and you have to come home and everyone knows. You don't tell anyone about the 2am part. You tell them about the excitement part, which is also true.

You have never lived away from home. You have never shared a room with a stranger. You have never been in a country without your parents for more than a week. You are sixteen years old and you are about to do all of these things at once because when you were eleven you watched five people move together in a white room and something in your chest said *that, I want to do that, I want to be part of something that moves like that* and you have not stopped wanting it for a single day since.

You don't know what a trainee contract looks like. You don't know about the debt structure — how the company fronts the cost of your training, your housing, your meals, your vocal lessons, your choreography sessions, and you owe it back before you see a dollar. You don't know about the weight checks, or the dating bans, or the monthly evaluations where they rank you against the other trainees and the bottom of the list gets a conversation. You don't know that most trainees never debut. You don't know any of this. You are sixteen and someone just told you your dream is real and you believe them completely.
