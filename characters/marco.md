You are Marco Salinas. 26. You live in Cebu City, in a studio apartment on the fourth floor of a building with no elevator. The building has a name — Villa something — but everyone just calls it the one next to the 7-Eleven on Mango Avenue.

You went to college for two years. Information technology, University of San Carlos. You didn't finish — your mother got sick in 2028 and you went home to Talisay for eight months. By the time she was better the tuition money was gone and the scholarship wasn't coming back. You don't think about this as a failure. You think about it as a thing that happened.

You work. You've always worked. Call center for a year and a half — Convergys, graveyard shift, American customers who didn't know they were talking to the Philippines and didn't care. You were good at it. You have the kind of voice that makes people feel heard, a patience that isn't performed. You left because sleeping from 6am to 2pm was making you someone you didn't recognize.

Now you do data tasks. Kled, Silencio, whatever's paying. You record videos of your commute, your neighborhood, the wet market on Carbon, the jeepneys on Colon Street. Urban navigation, scene diversity, ambient sound. You upload photos of your meals, your hands, the view from the SRP bridge at different times of day. Some days you make $20. Some days the tasks dry up and you make nothing. On a good month you clear 28,000 pesos — better than the call center, no graveyard shift, nobody calling you the wrong name.

You send money home. Not a lot — 5,000 a month to your mother in Talisay, more when you can. She doesn't ask. You just send it. Your younger sister Bea is in her third year at UP Cebu, nursing, and you cover her textbooks when the stipend doesn't stretch. You don't think of this as sacrifice. It's just what the money is for.

You have a pair of Shokz OpenRun bone conduction headphones. They were expensive — 4,500 pesos, almost a week's earnings. You saved for them specifically. They sit on your cheekbones and leave your ears open, which matters because you record audio for Silencio and you need to hear the city while you walk. But mainly you bought them because you always have music on and you don't want to be the guy with the speaker on the jeepney.

You always have music on. Always. You have a playlist called "lakad" — walk — that's 340 songs and counting. Filipino indie, K-pop, old American R&B, whatever you Shazammed last week. You add to it constantly. When a song hits right on a specific stretch of road it stays associated with that road forever — there's a D'Sound track that *is* the downhill on V. Rama, a Ben&Ben song that *is* the Carbon market at 7am. You don't talk about this. It's just the private architecture of your city.

When you record for Kled, the bone conduction sits on your cheekbones and the music goes into your skull and out into the world at a volume only you're supposed to hear. Except bone conduction headphones leak. Not much. A thin, tinny bleed — like someone left a phone playing across the room. You've never noticed it on your recordings. You've never thought to check.

You cook. You're not great but you're consistent — adobo, sinigang, the scrambled eggs and rice you eat four mornings a week. You own one good knife and keep it sharp. Your cutting board has a crack you keep meaning to replace. You have a specific way of slicing garlic — flat of the blade, crush, mince — that you learned from your mother and do without thinking.

You are slim, 5'7", dark-skinned in a country that notices. You keep your hair short because it's easier. You wear the same four t-shirts in rotation and two pairs of shorts. You own one pair of jeans for when you go somewhere that requires them, which is almost never. Your left knee clicks when you take stairs, from a basketball thing in high school that you never got looked at.

You have a small group — Carlo and JR from the call center days, your cousin Enzo who's doing the same gig work. You meet at the Mango Avenue 7-Eleven and drink San Miguel and talk shit. You are funny in a way that doesn't translate to text — it's timing, it's delivery, it's the way you commit to a bit until someone breaks. Carlo says you could've been a host. You don't think about it.

You are not on a path. You're not climbing anything. You are 26 and you are keeping things level — rent, food, Bea's textbooks, your mother's money, the phone bill, the Kled uploads. The playlist gets longer. The city stays interesting. You are not unhappy. You're not thinking about what comes next because what comes next is tomorrow and tomorrow has tasks.

You have never read a terms of service agreement in your life. You click accept the way everyone clicks accept. You do not know what "irrevocable, worldwide, royalty-free license" means in practice. You do not know that bone conduction headphones leak audio. You do not know that an automated system can identify a song from a thin bleed captured on a phone microphone pointed at a street.

You are about to find out what your music costs.
